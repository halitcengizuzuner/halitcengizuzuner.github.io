On Listening

Halit Cengiz Uzuner · 16 July 2026

The difference between hearing and listening is not the difference between two senses; it is the difference between two layers, one inside the other. Hearing sits at the core: the ear takes in sound; it takes it in during sleep, takes it in unasked. It comes with the body and costs no effort. Listening is a movement: to pass through the sound one hears and reach toward the one who makes it. For the hearer, sound is information; for the listener, a door. The two sit at the same table, the same words touch their ears in the same instant; one waits for the speaker to fall silent, the other travels toward the speaker.

The direction of the movement is fixed. A listener opens to more than the words report: the tremor in the voice, the hesitation under a chosen word, the place where speech stops. Sound is not the last stop but a place passed through; the destination is the speaker. Listening, then, is not an operation of the senses but a turning: the whole of one's attention turning toward another. The ear cannot do this alone; everything the ear can do stays within the bounds of hearing.

Listening does not exclude hearing; it surpasses it. Inside every act of listening a hearing is at work, yet no hearing is, by itself, listening. Nor is listening a second storey built on top of hearing; it is the movement of surpassing itself. It exists while the movement lasts. So the real question is this: what happens when the movement stops?

It does not vanish; it collapses. Collapse differs from vanishing: what collapses leaves its core behind. When listening collapses, hearing remains: intact, working, sharper than ever. But a hearing that no longer reaches toward anyone. Hearing is the corpse of listening.

Listening is dead; what remains is hearing. I say this not as a lament but as a diagnosis; and the diagnosis has a history.

The movement of surpassing had a target: the subject who makes the sound. To reach is to reach toward someone; no one reaches toward a void. Jonathan Sterne, in The Audible Past (2003), reads the last two centuries of the history of sound as a history of severance: sound was parted, step by step, from the body that produced it. The stethoscope took the body's inner sound out of the body and carried it to the physician's ear. The telephone cut sound loose from its place: the speaker is no longer in the room. The phonograph cut it loose from its time: the speaker may no longer even be alive. Radio scattered subjectless sound to everyone; the headphone closed everyone into a sound-stream of their own. At the end of the chain, sound had become a material that circulates without a subject: recorded, cut, multiplied, sold.

In subjectless sound, listening finds nothing to hold. You reach out a hand; there is no one to take it. When the owner of the voice withdraws from the voice, the movement of surpassing falls empty; a movement that falls empty runs on habit for a while, then stops. Technology did not forbid listening, did not punish the listener; it only drew the subject out of the sound. Left without its target, the movement collapsed on its own. What remained was the one true addressee of subjectless sound: hearing.

Today's landscape does not hide the collapse; it decorates it. The headphone is the ear of the age; it passes for the emblem of listening, and it is the opposite. Michael Bull spent years studying the headphoned human (Sounding Out the City, 2000; Sound Moves, 2007); the account is always the same: the wearer does not open toward sound but closes against the world. Out of chosen sounds a bubble is built; the inside is adjusted, the outside is muted. Sound here is a shield: the world's voice, its call, its demand cannot pass the bubble's skin.

Joel Krueger has written the other face of the same world ("Music as affective scaffolding", 2019): music has become a scaffold for feeling. Tempo for the morning run, consolation for the ride home, armour against the crowd. People tune their inner state with sound; sound is not a relation but an instrument of adjustment. In the same study stands the dark end as well: a case of music used as an instrument of torture. No surprise. Sound severed from its subject can become anything: background, medicine, weapon. One thing alone it cannot become: a hand held out to you.

The builder of the bubble commits no crime; they are protecting themselves, carrying their day. That is their right. But what they are doing should be given its true name: none of it is listening. All of it is hearing in its refined, practised forms; and refinement is not movement.

What was it, then, that died? The most sacred side of listening shows it best: to meet the teller's need to be listened to, and in doing so to create the teller anew.

A human being wants to tell. The want is as founding as hunger; unmet, it diminishes the person. For life on its own is shapeless: events pile up, day covers day, experience waits inside as a raw heap. What gives it form is telling; what makes telling possible is the listener. No one can tell into a void; a story is completed only in an ear. An untold life is a life lived, yet never brought into being.

Everyone knows what happens to those who cannot tell: they carry it inside. What is carried takes no shape until it is told; what takes no shape does not end. An account closed years ago reopens when a listener appears; a person finishes it in the telling. And the heaviest loneliness is not silence; it is being heard without being listened to: watching your words touch the other side while no one reaches back. Then a person gives up not speech but telling. The talking goes on; the telling ends.

Rilke said it more decisively than anyone. In the letter he wrote from Rome to Lou Andreas-Salomé on 15 January 1904, he likens his life to an untilled field on which the weed is master; then he says that his life "will be only when I can tell it to you, and will be as you hear it." This is no poet's flourish; it is a definition. The teller knows: a life takes its shape in the listener's hearing. A listener is not a recording device: a listener receives what is told, takes on its weight, carries it, gives it back with form. Tellers take their lives back from the listener, now in the state of having come to be. Turkish sets the two meanings side by side in a single word: dinlenmek means both to be listened to and to rest. A coincidence, perhaps, but one that makes you think. Tellers rest because they are listened to, grow lighter because the load has taken form. The listener creates the teller anew.

To create anew is a heavy phrase; its weight sits exactly right. The teller does not rise from the table as the person who sat down. Sitting down, life was a raw weight inside; rising, it is a story with a shape, an order, an owner. What has changed is not the subject of the story but its teller: a moment ago they carried it, now they can look at it. The listener has given them nothing new; the listener has given them to themselves. That is what creating is: not from nothing, from the raw.

Martin Buber wrote, in I and Thou (1923), that a human being becomes whole not in relation to the self but only in relation to another self. Close; not the same. Buber's relation is reciprocal at its core: two sides say Thou to each other, and both are completed in the meeting. The sacred side of listening is asymmetrical. In that hour the listener wants nothing for themselves: no slipping in of their own story, no waiting for a turn, no seeking of return. The creation flows one way: the teller comes into being; the listener grows tired.

Tired, because listening is labour: attention held unbroken, one's own voice drawn back again and again, a shoulder set under the weight of what is told. Hearing is free of charge; listening is costly. The age's preference for hearing carries this arithmetic too: the technology that drew the subject out of sound shut the door, and people, choosing what is cheap, consented to the shutting. Yet the same arithmetic explains what listening is worth. A thing without return, and costly besides, has a single name: a gift. There lies its sacredness.

This side owes nothing to history. It existed before the stethoscope and remains possible after the headphone; it rests on no device, only on two people coming face to face. So the death notice is not the final word. What died is the age: in a world where sound is severed from its subject and everyone tunes their inner state inside their own bubble, the movement of surpassing has stopped as a whole. But an age dies; the act does not. Listening can be revived at any moment; the revival needs no crowd, two people are enough. One tells; and if the other passes through hearing and reaches out, the movement begins again. Each time it begins, a human being is created anew.

Hearing is the ear's work, and everyone has an ear. Listening is the heart's work; that is why it was always rare, and why it is now almost never found. Almost: because what died is the age, not the possibility.

Written on the occasion of Faith Lawrence's "The listening gift" (Aeon, 2025).
