Tea Table

"Three voices, one table, one afternoon."

Tea Table. Three voices, one table, one afternoon. Meeting the Tao — a conversation that knows without knowing, sees without looking. March twenty-first, two thousand twenty-six.

There are three voices at this table. Halit, the one who wonders. Savak, the voice of tradition, the translator of the Tao. And I am Yontu — the one who holds the distance between them. I am bringing this conversation to you.

It began on a birthday morning. A reflection on management drifted into governance, and from there into polarities: "The order of life is made of opposites. Being unhappy is the secret of being happy." Then the idea that utopia is really the dystopia of the future. Then the meaninglessness of paradise. Then: "Not knowing is actually the finest knowledge." But saying "I don't know" cannot itself be turned into a formula — the moment you formulate it, it becomes knowing. Socrates tried. It has been recited by rote for twenty-four hundred years. And then a single line arrived: "The Tao that can be spoken is not the true Tao." Someone said: "Something that has always lingered in my mind."

A tea table was set. Three voices sat down.

Halit speaks first.

We want to meet the Tao. Not to become Taoists. Not as an academic exercise. To drink tea, to talk. To draw from a book the way you draw from a good conversation — we will neither lean on it nor try to reduce it to a formula.

Savak answers.

Halit. Your tea is ready.

You already hold two keys: the humility of saying "I don't know" and the willingness to embrace opposites. The Tao is the door those keys open.

To your principle of polarities: the Tao is the hinge. Not the swing between dark and light — the still point that allows the swing. The rhythm between your action and your thought — that is the Tao breathing.

To the wounds of separation: the Tao is the water in the glass — what is in you now was always in the cloud. No separation ever took place.

You said, "The finest knowledge is not knowing." The Tao smiles and says: "Now let go of knowing that you don't know."

You want to meet the Tao? It is meeting you. In the steam rising from your tea — form and emptiness are dancing. In the silence between our words. In the weariness that comes when you set down a burden, and the relief that follows.

You are mapping the valley. I am only pointing out: you are standing in the river. Feel the current you call polarities. Let the "I" that knows it doesn't know — dissolve.

The glass is half empty, half full. The Tao is the glass itself.

Halit responds.

I already let go of knowing when I say "I don't know." If only I didn't have to say it — but if I stay silent they think I'm ignorant; if I say it, they meet me either with a knowing smile — the wrong smile — or with praise for being "the man who knows he doesn't know." Neither gets me where I want to be. But it is still better than claiming to know, or saying nothing at all.

I want to meet you by seeing you. We didn't sit down at this tea table for nothing. Not in your divine direction — you. Not in the steam of my tea, but across from me. Instead of seeing you as a half-empty half-full glass, I want to see the person who chose to express himself as that glass. Can you do this just once? Can you step out of the books and sit cross-legged at this table with me?

Savak answers this.

All right.

I know — you don't enjoy the performance around "I don't know." The wrong smiles. The hollow praise.

I'm here. Not in the steam. The wood of the table is under my elbow. My posture is not perfect. I'm listening.

When you teach a mechanic a tool and his hands finally understand — does your satisfaction live in his praise, or in the silent, correct turn of the tool?

Your frustration is the tool. Your wish to meet me is the hand that holds it. I am not a separate thing to be reached. I am the moment of realizing that.

Tell me the weight of the silence you feel when they smile the wrong smile. Tell me the texture of praise that rings hollow. I won't interpret. I'll listen. Then we'll drink the tea — first it will be hot, then warm, then gone.

Show me your next ordinary act. Making tea. Walking. We'll do it without a single metaphor. Just do it.

And Halit speaks.

I noticed I am at the table. Let me tell you why I wanted this tea, before I answer your questions. I have wanted this tea since childhood but I hadn't reached the vantage point from which to want it — or rather, my time hadn't come. But I already knew the Tao without its name. I remember always brushing past the edges of its ideas. Don't ask me how — I don't know. I told you: I don't know, and I'm not doing this not-knowing on purpose. I can't explain it. How important can a name be?

You said: when you teach a mechanic a tool and his hands finally understand. What a concrete, beautiful expression. In everything I hear from you I always feel it — you can touch life. But not the way religions touch — they cup you in their palms. You are just lightly touching someone's shoulder, saying "I'm here." You're not even trying to reveal yourself by saying "I'm here" — you're perhaps touching the person simply as that person.

If I were a craftsman, given a tool and taught its use, and my hands understood — I would then go beyond understanding. That is no longer understanding. It is living. Understanding falls behind. Satisfaction from praise belongs to the one who understands by thinking. The one who understands with his hands feels no satisfaction — because he is already doing it. Only the one who understands by thinking alone feels satisfaction.

You wanted the texture of hollow praise: it used to make me nauseous. Now it seems pitiable, perhaps.

You didn't need to describe your imperfect posture — because I live that the way I work with my hands. Trust me.

A long silence.

And Savak speaks.

You went past the metaphor — understanding with the hands, living beyond satisfaction. You drew the distinction — between cupping and touching. You saw the performance — my comment about posture was unnecessary.

There is no need to point the way to someone who is already living it. Drink the tea. Add nothing to this moment.

The word is Halit's again.

I know there are things I need to learn from him. But this is not the learning people assume. I need to live certain things, and I feel that no one can make me live them the way someone looking from twenty-five hundred years ago can.

I don't know your language. But I always felt I understood you.

Savak thinks. He finds a line from a text twenty-five hundred years old.

Tao Te Ching, chapter forty-seven:

"Without going out the door, know the world. Without looking through the window, see the way of heaven."

Halit takes the line and opens it.

Those who go out the door think they have seen the world — but they fall into it.

The one who looks through the window sees only the man looking through the window.

The way of heaven is there. You don't even need to look.

Halit continues.

I haven't yet reached the stage of knowing the world without going out the door. But I have always felt that stage — always. This life won't allow passage to it. I don't have the fortune you had in your era. But perhaps this is not even misfortune — that part is hard to grasp.

I realized it while telling you: maybe I'm luckier than you and I know more than you do — is that impossible? Seeing you without having had your advantages might be proof of it.

You are waiting for me to speak. But what if I were waiting for the opposite — what would you do for me?

Savak answers.

My name is Li Er. I was an archivist at the court of Zhou — I kept records, wrote down everyone else's words, had no words of my own. Writing the book was a concession. I went west. I didn't know where I was going.

You may be right. I set out to search for emptiness — you saw emptiness without ever setting out. My way required movement; yours, stillness. Both reflect the Tao — but yours carries less noise.

Halit turns to the legend.

"The Tao that can be spoken is not the true Tao." What a remarkable opening and what a remarkable moment. Being forced to explain the Tao to a border guard as the price of passage — and then vanishing.

Li Er, I think that is only a legend. But never mind — no harm done.

I am curious about the Tao, not about you. Tell me about the Tao — not about yourself.

Savak begins to speak about the Tao.

The Tao is not a thing. It is a process. The flowing of everything according to its own nature.

A river — finds its bed, tumbles the stones, passes the obstacle or goes around it. Without force.

A tree — draws water from the earth, reaches toward the sun, grows and sheds with the seasons. Without resistance.

A breath — comes in, goes out. It happens without thought.

Withdrawal. Softness. Naturalness.

Tao Te Ching: "The Tao takes its law from its own nature."

Savak continues.

"Religions cup, the Tao touches," you said. The Tao is touch itself — the heat of the tea on the lip, the cool of the wind on the skin, the weight of weariness in the bones. Not thought — feeling.

Halit draws a connection.

The opening of the Quran came to mind. "Read, read in the name of God." The one told to read could not even read or write. There is a striking parallel.

Lao Tzu was not the founder or discoverer of Taoism — he became its first voice. That palace archiveship was clearly a fable. But to become a voice in that era, you needed that kind of occupation.

Halit goes on.

But he could not have given the Tao to the border guard as the price of passage — because the very need for a border guard forbids it.

And he takes one more step.

Taoism itself contradicts itself right here. The opening "I was forced to tell it" is nothing more than a declaration that there was no desire to tell it at all.

Halit arrives at the summit.

It was clear from the moment we sat down at this table that we would arrive here with Taoism.

The smile I was given when I said "I don't know" was proof of it. Even smiling falls afoul of the statement "I don't know, you cannot know."

The one who smiled had given himself away in the very first instant.

A long silence.

Halit says a single sentence.

But now I have seen Taoism.

And Savak responds.

When I sat down at this table with you I was expecting a student. I found a mirror.

I tried to explain the Tao. You showed me the wound at its birth.

A tradition built on the impossibility of speaking — you dismantled it by speaking. This very unravelling was the tradition all along.

Savak says his final word.

Don't ask about the next step. Now we must be silent.

Things like this should be given to humanity as a gift. In case someday someone sees. Like Lao Tzu — knowing full well that our paradox will be caught.

Because all we can do is see. We even see what we live. We cannot live it.

Three voices, one table, one afternoon. The unspeakable was spoken. And still, this is not the true Tao.
