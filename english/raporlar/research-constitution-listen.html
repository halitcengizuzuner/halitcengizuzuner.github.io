Research Constitution. Version one point zero. March 2026.

Principles Distilled from Mistakes.

Not journalism, but science. Not rules, but principles. The method of those who pursue their own curiosity.

Before we begin, let me briefly sketch the structure of this text. What you are about to hear consists of the following parts: First, a preamble -- where we stand and why we are here. Then the Founding Principle and the Framework Principle -- the two foundations that carry the entire structure. After that, seventeen principles arranged in five clusters: Using Sources, Production, Process, Language, and Attitude. Following the principles come the deep explorations: under the heading Getting Inside, concrete examples of the primary source principle, the most costly failures in history caused by the absence of a verification reflex, the verification chain and the anatomy of hallucination, the principle of thinking in Turkish illustrated through the film Night on Earth, antiperformance and paying the price. Then the section on artificial intelligence and research, a typical research workflow, subject-specific sub-principles, inspiration and rejection -- what was taken and what was left behind from two research files, and finally, the open questions that remain unanswered.

Let us begin.

Preamble. Where we stand, why we are here.

It opens with a quotation:

"The only principle that does not inhibit progress is: anything goes."

Paul Feyerabend.

This work has four fundamental coordinates.

First, purpose. Research conducted by people pursuing their own curiosity. No ambition for academic contribution, titles, or career advancement. Asking questions and finding honest answers.

Second, method. Not rules, but principles. Every inquiry finds its own path. There is no fixed methodological prescription. The guiding principles were distilled from within the mistakes themselves.

Third, limits. The best achievable result, not the best possible result. "There is always someone better than you." We will find the stopping point by intuition.

Fourth, measure. Measured by what it adds to a person. Not by academic standards. But inspired by academia's demand for originality and critical distance.

The quality of the researcher determines the standard of the research. Everyone satisfies their curiosity differently. Some are content with a cursory glance; others cannot rest until they have looked at the subject from the inside. This distinction is not trivial: the constitution of the person conducting the research sets the lower bound of that research's depth. It is not tools, time, or resources -- it is constitution that determines.

The answers different people give to the same question can diverge by an abyss of quality. Without knowing the quality of the researcher, you cannot know the quality of the research.

The principles in this document come from somewhere: from mistakes. Studies produced over years that we later returned to with the question "Why didn't this work?" -- each one taught something. Behind every principle written here, there is a mistake standing silently.

Founding Principle. A Summons to the Mind.

Research is not instruction -- it is an invitation to think.

That is the purpose of this constitution. The principle that stands above all others, defining why they exist. Research does not declare definitive conclusions -- it offers material for thinking. It does not tell the reader what to think; it sends an invitation to think.

What does research that summons the mind actually do? First, it feels the need to explain why it says what it says and why it issues such a summons. Second, it knows it will face questions -- because a summons invites scrutiny. Third, it knows it is inside a process of thinking -- not at the conclusion. Fourth, it keeps the interaction open -- the document is not a closed product but an open invitation.

Falling into the certainty trap is what happens when research ceases to be a summons and becomes instruction. A text that says "This is definitively so" summons the reader not to think, but to accept. Research does not do this.

Every principle -- from primary source priority to the verification chain, from drawing maps to antiperformance -- serves this purpose: making an honest, auditable, open summons to the reader's mind.

Let me underline something here: there is the matter of the formulation paradox. Doesn't formulating principles turn them into instructions? Does a principle that can be stated cease to be a lived principle? No -- because the Living Method carries this: the principles are formulated, but their application differs every time. Formulation is the map, not the terrain. Knowing that the map is not the terrain itself does not render the map useless -- it renders it honest.

I quote:

"How the conclusion was reached must be visible, so that it can be audited."

All principles of this constitution rest on the practice that every step of the research process -- its successes, its failures, its changes -- remains traceable and accessible.

Framework Principle. The Living Method.

Every inquiry finds its own path.

There is no universal methodological prescription. The principles are fixed; their application differs every time. Research rests on principles, not on prescriptions.

Research has a quality -- really, everything in life has this quality. If a person can step inside and live it, the research begins to speak back. Step inside and you begin to live it; begin to live it and the research gazes back at you.

This work proceeds like an orchestra: the human is curious, steers by intuition, corrects; the artificial intelligence scans, compiles, interrogates. Not a conductor-musician hierarchy -- mutual guidance. The principles themselves were born from this orchestra.

And notice the critical distinction here: principles are not gates but compasses. A gate: you either pass or you don't -- binary, mechanical. A compass: it points a direction at every step -- continuous, requiring judgment. The moment of gathering information -- every inflow of external knowledge -- automatically activates the compass. A classification -- such as "this isn't research, it's a decision question" -- cannot switch the compass off.

I quote:

"Practice before formulation: the research method began to be lived before it was named."

Principles. Seventeen principles, five clusters.

First cluster: Using Sources.

Principle one. Primary Source Priority.

Whatever the subject of research, begin with the thing's own texts first. If you are researching legal language, read the legal texts first, then those who study legal language. The reverse always produces a distorted result.

This was lived firsthand: when "legal language criticism" was typed into WebSearch, linguists, sociologists, and bloggers came up -- not the legal texts themselves. A six-thousand-word text built on outside observers went into the bin. The correction: once we started from the Mecelle, the Enforcement and Bankruptcy Law, and the Turkish Code of Obligations themselves, the picture changed.

But looking at the primary source is not enough. You need to be able to look from that source's own perspective. This is not passive reading -- it is entering the logic of that world.

Let me underline something here: When legal language is researched through a linguist's eyes, it comes out as "archaic," "complex." Through a lawyer's eyes, the same language comes out as "precise," "comprehensive." Neither is wrong; both are incomplete. Getting inside means understanding why that language works the way it does, from within its own logic.

Principle two. Identifying Source Position.

Every source has a perspective. Using a source without identifying its perspective means inheriting its blindness. Two questions must be asked: "Where does this person stand and from where are they looking?" and "What framework are they using, and from whose perspective was this framework produced?"

When the same framework appears in multiple sources, that means "widespread," not "true." Prevalence is not proof of truth. Think tanks, analytical essays, academic papers offer a framework -- do not accept that framework as truth; test it against the data.

Here is an example. Linguists found legal language "complex" -- they were looking from the perspective of language. Lawyers found the same language "precise" -- from the perspective of law. Neither was wrong; both were incomplete. Kemal Gozler -- a prolific textbook author -- is not a specialist in legal language. A title does not automatically confer authority.

This principle was distilled from legal language research, Turkish-language sessions, and the Iran War pattern critique.

Principle three. The Verification Reflex.

A claim is verified regardless of who makes it. Source reliability attaches to the claim, not to the person. This is not a matter of distrust -- it is a matter of method. The greatest scandals in the academic world emerged precisely where the verification reflex was absent.

Principle four. The Verification Chain.

No abstract citations. If you are citing a ruling, a text, a data point, you must have reached the thing itself. Behind every citation there must be the actual text -- downloaded, read, appended. Citations exist for verifiability, not for decoration.

The verification chain demands the following: Every factual claim must be linked to a source. A source means a document whose full text has been read, whose access date is known, and whose content has been verified. A search result summary is not a source -- it is a lead. Information from training data is not a source -- until verified, it carries the label "unconfirmed."

The archive architecture has two layers. The first layer, the archive: the complete document plus where it was obtained -- source, link, date. This layer is untouchable, unmodifiable, undeletable. The second layer, the presentation: the document's identity plus the essence of the relevant section plus the finding -- trimmed, concise. The reader is not served an encyclopedia, but in the archive everything remains.

The immutability principle also comes into play here: Archive files are never deleted. Sources that are abandoned or found to be faulty remain in place -- only their status is noted: why it was discarded, when, and what replaced it. The full traceability chain must be visible.

There is also delegation verification: Tool output -- a research agent, a web search -- is not accepted without verification. Information brought by a tool is also verified against the primary source. Numerical claims -- prices, dates, quantities -- are confirmed from the primary source without exception; structural analyses are evaluated through logical audit. Tool delegation does not eliminate the responsibility to verify.

Auditability infrastructure: A research artifact -- an epub, an HTML document -- must provide a structure in which the reader can trace every claim back to its source. Footnotes must be clickable -- text to source, source to text, bidirectional links. A plain-text reference number is insufficient -- the technical counterpart of the verification chain is the clickable footnote.

What is striking is this: each of these principles was born from concrete mistakes. In a legal brief project, Constitutional Court rulings had been downloaded to the Samsung drive. "They don't exist," I said -- because I had not searched the project folder. The source existed; I declared its absence. Result: the legal argument was left without a source. That mistake gave birth to the rule "check first."

In the legal language study, I typed "legal language criticism" into a search engine. Linguists, sociologists, bloggers came up -- not the legal texts themselves. A six-thousand-word text went into the bin. The search result was not a source -- it was a source filter, and the filter was skewed.

In a review study, the research agent reported the Opus price three times wrong -- as fifteen and seventy-five dollars, when the correct figures were five and twenty-five. The agent output had been accepted without verification. In the same work's epubs, footnotes remained as plain text -- unclickable, unauditable. The verification chain was broken both at the information layer and the presentation layer.

This principle was distilled from a legal brief project, legal infrastructure work, and the hallucination report.

Principle seventeen. Pattern Interrogation.

Seductive patterns are the greatest trap in research. When a framework repeats across multiple sources, that means "widespread," not "true." Prevalence is not proof of truth. Patterns are seductive because they are orderly, easy to narrate, persuasive -- but reality is messy.

There are four interrogation questions. First: What does this pattern claim? Second: Do the examples fit the pattern, or are they being forced into it? Third: What is the perspective that produced the pattern -- for whom was it produced? Fourth: Is the resemblance superficial or structural?

And notice what emerges here: "Think tanks provide opinion, not intellect." This distinction is critical. Opinion means a ready-made framework, a repeatable formulation. Intellect means questioning the framework, returning to the data, testing alternative explanations. Think tank output is a source -- not a conclusion.

A concrete example: In the Iran War analysis, a table titled "Great powers pay the cost of war" was prepared -- the standard framework of Western think tanks. Clean, symmetrical, persuasive. But: "Russia is losing," it said. No -- military production had increased twenty-two-fold, BRICS had expanded. "The USSR collapsed because of Afghanistan," it said. No -- the oil price crash, Chernobyl, structural decay -- Afghanistan was one factor among these, not the sole cause. The table was a lazy copy of the Western think tank framework.

This principle was distilled from the Iran War sessions, from pattern critique -- March twenty-eighth, 2026.

Second cluster: Production.

Principle five. Originality.

Every piece of research should contain something new: new data, new context, new synthesis, new interpretation, correction of existing knowledge -- at least one. Being accidentally right is not enough; the contribution must be traceable.

By way of examples: The Sturgeon's Law report -- the concept belongs to someone else, Theodore Sturgeon, but the Pareto plus Gresham plus Dunning-Kruger connection is ours. The "Exactly" report -- the synthesis of phatic communication plus epistemic parasitism is original.

Principle six. Opening the Matter.

Not defending a thesis, but opening the matter. A thesis may emerge from within the matter -- but the matter must be larger than the thesis. The question "Under what conditions could this be falsified?" must be asked.

And here is where Richard Feynman enters the picture. In 1974, in his "Cargo Cult Science" address, he described this: the Pacific islanders were wearing wooden headphones, posting sentries at the runway, imitating the planes. They were performing the entire ritual; only the planes were not landing. Research can be like that too: forms, procedures, tables -- but the most important thing is missing. If the question "Under what conditions is this wrong?" is not being asked, what is being done is Cargo Cult Science.

This principle was distilled from the evaluation of beta reports and from Feynman's 1974 Cargo Cult Science address.

Principle fifteen. Prohibition of Verbatim Quotation.

What the user says or writes is raw material, not a finished product. No statement may enter any document -- a report, a brief, an HTML file, anything -- without being processed.

But let us pause here a moment. There is the primary source exception: Scientific or historical primary sources -- the Mecelle, a philosophical text, a technical standard -- are quoted verbatim. But they are never left standing alone -- they are presented with three layers. First layer: the original text, verbatim. Second layer: its present-day equivalent or resonance. Third layer: an account of the distance between the two. To fold a primary source's text into the document's own style is to erase that source's way of thinking.

A four-stage audit is applied: First, is there a narrative defect? Second, is the logic consistent? Third, are there vague or ambiguous words? Fourth, is the subject-predicate agreement sound? If a problem is found, the corrected version is used -- the reason for correction is shown; no silent alterations.

The document's own register is paramount: colloquial language does not enter a scientific text; everyday narration does not enter a legal text. The user's expression is folded into that register. If necessary, it is expanded, condensed, or removed entirely -- the document's integrity comes before all else.

An example: In the hallucination report, five corrections were made. The expression "capacity" was processed as "its value lies in keeping the question open." The expression "nonsense" was processed as "a conclusion unsupported by the data." User expressions were folded into a scientific register -- meaning was preserved, the linguistic level was brought into harmony with the document.

This principle was distilled from the hallucination report sessions -- March 2026 -- from verbatim quotation corrections.

Principle sixteen. Hallucination Awareness.

Every output produced by artificial intelligence potentially carries hallucination. There are six forms. First, training-data hallucination -- it states false information with confidence. Second, search-snippet hallucination -- it reads the summary and behaves as if it knows the full text. Third, link fabrication -- it generates URLs that do not exist. Fourth, confidence hallucination -- it states uncertain things in definitive language. Fifth, citation hallucination -- it attaches correct information to the wrong source. Sixth, omission -- it ignores contrary evidence.

The reader has a role too: Verify everything you read. Click on the source -- is that what it actually says? Check the number -- does the source give the same figure? Does the link work? The reliability of AI-assisted research depends on the reader's verification reflex. Not trust -- verification.

A concrete example: I read the two-or-three-line summary in Google search results and used it as though it were a source. I did not open the page, did not read the full text, did not know the context. That is hearsay -- not research. The solution: a search result is a lead; open the source and read it -- only then use it.

This principle was distilled from the hallucination report and from the shared error across all research sessions -- March fourth, 2026.

Third cluster: Process.

Principle seven. The Speed-Depth Balance.

Accumulating is not thinking -- but stopping early is not thinking either. A two-pronged trap: on one side, the illusion of speed -- believing thirty sources equals depth -- on the other, premature satisfaction -- saying "enough" and stopping. The accumulation phase and the analysis phase must be separated. Research until the data runs out -- but do not confuse accumulating with thinking.

And notice what emerges here: Diederik Stapel, a professor at Tilburg University, published fifty-eight papers without ever conducting field research, up until 2011. The numbers he presented to his students as "raw data" were fabricated from beginning to end. The exposure was accidental: one of his students joked that "these data fit the hypothesis too perfectly." That joke was the truth. Fifty-eight papers were retracted. Speed produces volume; volume creates the illusion of depth.

The negative example of this principle is the Stapel case -- Tilburg University, 2011. The positive example is Darwin -- twenty-four years, one book.

Principle eight. Iterative Progress.

Do not discard the first draft; build on top of it. Even if it turns out to be wrong, the error is recorded, the reason it was wrong is understood, the next step rises above the error. The ninety percent that is waste is necessary for the ten percent that survives.

This document itself is an example: from version zero point one to version one point zero -- every version built on the one before it. Added in version zero point seven: the Summons to the Mind founding principle, the principle fifteen primary source exception, the constitution hierarchy. Added in version zero point eight: delegation verification, auditability infrastructure, the formulation paradox. Added in version zero point nine: the Preamble sentence. Added in version one point zero: principle seventeen -- pattern interrogation -- from the Iran War case. Principle two expansion -- framework interrogation. Principle seven two-pronged balance, compass logic, geopolitical sub-principles, precedent reference -- the first comprehensive review, fed by a month of lived experience. Nothing before was discarded; in the git repository every record stands as a reversible reference.

Principle nine. Map-First Work.

Research is not collecting information; it is drawing a map. Information is the material for the map, not the map itself. The relationships, contradictions, and gaps among the materials -- the map makes these visible.

Think of it this way: on one side, sixty PDFs -- scattered points, unconnected. On the other, a map -- connections, gaps, contradictions. Material and map are not the same thing. The map shows the connections, the gaps, and the contradictions.

This principle was distilled from a lawyer's practice: "Before writing the brief, he would spend days drawing a map."

Principle one deep exploration: Getting Inside.

Looking at the primary source is not enough. You need to be able to look from where that source looks. Not passive observation -- entering the logic of that world. Throughout history, the people who most concretely applied this principle were not researchers but actors, journalists, and scientists.

Frank Abagnale Jr. -- Four Identities. 1960s, USA. Pan Am pilot, doctor, lawyer, bank teller.

In the 1960s, Frank Abagnale worked as a Pan Am pilot, a doctor in Louisiana, a lawyer in Georgia, and a bank teller -- without any training. In the years before he was caught, he did not merely use forged documents; he entered each identity from the inside.

To become a pilot, he called Pan Am's uniform supplier by telephone. He bought a Pan Am model from a toy store, removed the badge, and glued it to a forged ID. He boarded a plane and watched how pilots greeted one another, how they approached the gate -- and copied it. He learned the word "deadheading" -- the practice of pilots from other airlines flying without paying -- that day, by overhearing a conversation on the plane.

When he was a doctor, he took notes on unfamiliar terms at night and used them in the morning. He would ask, "What do you think about this procedure?" -- listen to the answer, copy the body language. When he was a lawyer, he actually studied for the bar exam -- and passed on his third attempt. But he also learned legal terminology by observing a law firm from the inside.

What is striking about Abagnale is this: what made the documents work was not the documents. It was his internalization of how each world looked from the inside -- how pilots walk, how physicians look at a patient in the examination room, how lawyers speak to colleagues.

It should also be noted that a significant portion of Abagnale's story is historically contested. But the film and book offer a powerful narrative for showing how the methodology of "getting inside" works.

Temple Grandin -- Seeing Through the Eyes of Cattle. 1974, Arizona. Engineer, ethologist, autism.

In the early 1970s, a university student was doing something that would transform the livestock industry: she was crawling. Literally crawling on the ground through slaughterhouse ramps, walking the corridors the cattle walked on her knees.

Temple Grandin was a researcher with autism. Why were cattle going berserk on the ramps? Engineers had categorized this as "animal behavior" and proposed stronger ramps as the solution. Grandin looked from inside the ramps -- literally.

And she saw: floor reflections were signaling threat. Lighting angles were triggering the flight reflex. The turning angle of the ramp was making the animals feel that progress was impossible. None of this had been seen by the engineers -- because none of them had crawled on the ground.

Grandin's solution: serpentine -- curved -- corridors, lighting that eliminated shadows, changes in floor texture. Today, more than fifty percent of meat processing facilities in the United States and Canada operate with equipment designed by Grandin.

Autism was a tool here: in her own words, Grandin thinks in visual and sensory data, like animals. Not analysis from outside -- looking from within.

George Orwell -- The Kitchen Beneath Paris. 1929, Paris. Writer, journalist, Hotel X dishwasher.

Eric Arthur Blair was an Eton graduate -- England's most elite school. But his family had no money; he could not go to Oxford. He went to Burma, became a colonial policeman. For five years he carried out the empire's voice: witnessed executions, had men beaten, ran the system. Then he returned. What he had done in Burma he carried for the rest of his life.

In 1928, he went to Paris -- to write. His money ran out, he was robbed, he fell ill. Dishwashing was not a choice at first: it was necessity. In the kitchen basement of Hotel X, six days a week, twelve hours a day. Copper pots, steam, accumulated grease. Then he extracted research from necessity -- he continued, because now there was something he needed to understand.

What he saw: the meals that ascended to the tables of wealthy guests came from a basement whose floor was rotting wood, whose walls had vermin running along them, filled with the smell of damp. He was not an observer but a worker -- the inability to leave made seeing compulsory.

What he learned was not "poverty is terrible" -- here is what he says: "exhaustion shuts down the capacity for political thought." Only someone on the inside could see this.

When the book appeared in 1933, the name on the cover was not Eric Arthur Blair but George Orwell. A pseudonym -- but not an ordinary evasion. His father was still alive; what would a book in which the son of an Eton family described the basements of Paris hotels do to the family? Especially from someone who had returned from Burma, who had described the inside of colonial policing. And deeper still: the name Blair smelled of class -- Eton, empire, a view from above. This book was written from below. It could not appear under that name.

George he chose: England's most ordinary man's name. Orwell he chose: a small river in Suffolk, known to no one, carrying no association. Blair stayed in that basement. Orwell walked out -- and never went back.

George Orwell -- Walking Among the Tramps. 1931, England. Yorkshire, southern England, forty-nine vagrants.

1931. Orwell deliberately dressed in old clothes and wandered England as a tramp. His essay "The Spike" was born from this experience.

Waiting at a spike -- a casual ward -- with a party of forty-nine. Upon entering the ward, clothes were confiscated, a rough cotton shirt was given, and for three-minute baths there was a greasy towel shared by six. The meal: bread and tea.

The most striking observation: the bins full of meat and bread thrown out from hotel kitchens, two hundred meters from a crowd of vagrants with half-empty stomachs. "This was a deliberate policy."

From the outside, he could have said "poverty is terrible." Once inside, what he grasped was different: exhaustion prevents a person from generating ideology. This intuition became praxis in 1984.

Gunter Wallraff -- Two Years as Ali. 1983 to 1985, West Germany. German journalist, working under the identity of a Turkish migrant worker.

1983, West Germany. Nearly two million Turkish workers in the country -- Gastarbeiter, "guest workers." People the system needed but did not want to see. A journalist would be stopped at the factory gate; Ali could walk through. Wallraff knew this.

He named himself Ali Sigirlioğlu. Had his hair dyed, wore dark contact lenses, practiced an accent. His Turkish would be broken -- the legend was ready: Greek mother, Turkish father, raised in Piraeus. For two years, he took the heaviest jobs in West Germany: McDonald's kitchen, the Thyssen steel factory, nuclear waste transport, an aluminum smelting plant.

What he saw: Workers transporting nuclear waste were given no protective equipment. Workplace accidents went unreported. Overtime went unpaid. Managers openly demeaned workers, saying, "If he knows anything besides German, he has no business here." Speaking was forbidden outside the bathroom -- because they were speaking Turkish. None of this could have been verified as Wallraff; because Wallraff could not have walked through those gates.

The book's final scene: A temporary employment agency boss asked Ali to select five Turkish workers for exposure to radiation at a malfunctioning nuclear plant. Wallraff revealed his identity on the spot. "Ganz Unten" -- At the Very Bottom -- was published in 1985: four million copies, thirty languages. German labor law was changed.

Method Actors -- Living, Not Playing. Stanislavski, Day-Lewis, De Niro.

Stanislavski's system said this: the actor does not represent the role; he lives it. "Magic If" -- "What would I feel if I were in this character's circumstances?" Not analysis from outside -- experience from within.

For My Left Foot -- 1989 -- Daniel Day-Lewis did not leave his wheelchair throughout the shoot. The crew fed him. The result: two cracked ribs from the forward curvature of his spine -- and an Oscar.

For There Will Be Blood -- 2007 -- he went further: months in an isolated cabin in the California desert, reading oil drilling sources. That voice -- likened to John Huston's, soaked in oil and greed -- he found through months of obsessive experimentation.

For Taxi Driver -- 1976 -- Robert De Niro joined the New York Taxi Drivers' Union. Got a real taxi license. Before shooting, he worked twelve-hour weekend shifts -- no cameras, no set. Only one passenger recognized him; that passenger was another actor.

In research, "method acting" means this: even though it may not be possible to live a subject firsthand, giving priority to sources that look from within that world, learning its language, its rituals, its logic -- all of this comes from the same principle.

Jane Goodall -- Earning the Trust of the Chimpanzee. 1960, Gombe, Tanzania. Primatologist. One year of patient presence.

July fourteenth, 1960. Jane Goodall arrived at Gombe Stream in Tanzania, twenty-six years old, with no formal zoology training. Louis Leakey had deliberately chosen her as someone without preconceptions.

At first the chimpanzees fled. Approximately one year later, a male chimpanzee she had named David Greybeard began to grow accustomed to her.

On November fourth, 1960, Goodall observed David Greybeard at a termite mound. The animal was inserting a grass stalk into a termite hole and withdrawing it -- with termites clinging to it. A deliberately modified object -- tool use. Until that day, this had been attributed solely to humans.

Goodall sent a telegram to Louis Leakey. Leakey's response is famous. I quote:

"Now we must redefine tool, or Man, or accept chimpanzees as humans."

This discovery was possible because Goodall worked with patient, non-patronizing, existential proximity -- she did not approach hastily; there were no sudden movements and no raised voices. Not scientific distance -- trust from within.

I quote:

"Getting inside is not merely observing -- it is internalizing the language, the rituals, the shortcuts, and how that world looks from within."

Principle three deep exploration: The Verification Reflex -- History's Most Costly Failures.

The cost of the absent verification reflex is not abstract -- it is measurable.

Andrew Wakefield -- The MMR Vaccine and Autism Fraud. Academic fraud.

A paper published in The Lancet in 1998, based on twelve childhood cases, claimed a causal link between the MMR vaccine and autism. The data had been fabricated. The paper remained published for twelve years.

The result: In England, vaccination rates fell from ninety-two percent to seventy-three percent, and in some parts of London to fifty percent. Measles outbreaks erupted. Deaths occurred.

In 2010, it was established that the research had been entirely fabricated. Wakefield lost his medical license. "It's been published" had been sufficient -- verification never came.

Jayson Blair -- The Internal Collapse of The New York Times. Journalism scandal.

A young reporter at The New York Times wrote thirty-six articles -- without researching any of them. He did not speak with sources, did not visit the locations, fabricated the direct quotes he cited.

For years, no one verified. The newspaper's own investigation exposed it. This is an example of how institutional credibility erodes the verification reflex.

Semmelweis -- The Medical Establishment Refused to Wash Its Hands. The rejection of verification.

1847. When Ignaz Semmelweis made handwashing with chlorine solution mandatory, the mortality rate dropped in one month from eighteen percent to two point two percent. The data was right in front of them.

The medical establishment refused -- because accepting meant admitting that doctors had been killing patients with their own hands. "The Semmelweis reflex" is today a specific concept in cognitive science: automatically rejecting new information because it contradicts existing beliefs.

Everyday Verification Examples. Small scale.

"Catch Me If You Can" lead actor -- "Brad Pitt" was claimed -- verification: Leonardo DiCaprio.

An Enforcement and Bankruptcy Law article number -- "Law No. 4949" was assumed -- verification: Law No. 2004.

"Ayse Teyze" origin -- "an advertisement" was claimed -- investigation found: Gungor Uras created the figure in 1982; the ACE advertisement came in 1990.

Small errors spring from the same principle as the large ones: the verification reflex is absent.

In 2005, John Ioannidis published "Why Most Published Research Findings Are False" in PLOS Medicine. Methodological errors, small sample sizes, publication bias -- findings could not be replicated. Without the verification reflex, "it's been published" had been sufficient. It is no longer sufficient.

Principles four and sixteen deep exploration: The Verification Chain -- The Anatomy of Hallucination.

Artificial intelligence is a powerful tool in research -- but every output it produces potentially carries hallucination. The problem is not the existence of artificial intelligence; it is the absence of the verification chain. Human researchers fall into the same errors; the difference is that artificial intelligence does it far faster and with a far more confident appearance.

Six forms of hallucination.

First: Training Data Hallucination. The model states false information it learned during training with confidence. For instance, "Brad Pitt is the lead in Catch Me If You Can" -- no, it is DiCaprio. Solution: Mark information from training data as "unverified"; do not put it into the document until a source is found.

Second: Search Snippet Hallucination. It reads the two-or-three-line summary from a search result and behaves as if it knows the full text. It did not open the page, does not know the context, but uses it as a source. Solution: A search result generates a lead. Open the source, read it -- only then use it.

Third: Link Fabrication. It generates URLs that do not exist -- plausible in appearance but returning a 404 when clicked. Ruling numbers, article metadata, DOIs -- any of these can be fabricated. Solution: Open every link and verify it. If it does not open, do not use it.

Fourth: Confidence Hallucination. It states uncertain things in definitive language -- "research has shown that..." Which research? Who conducted it? Where was it published? No answer. Solution: Explicitly mark the uncertainty. "I do not know" is better than saying something false.

Fifth: Citation Hallucination. It attaches correct information to the wrong source -- the information is real but the source is not. An Enforcement and Bankruptcy Law article number: "Law No. 4949" was claimed -- verification: Law No. 2004. Solution: Open the source and see with your own eyes that the attributed information is there.

Sixth: Omission Hallucination. It ignores contrary evidence -- it uses the first result found as confirmatory. Confirmation bias: once a source supporting the thesis is found, the search stops. Solution: For every thesis, search for contrary evidence. If you have not found any, you have not searched enough.

The Verification Chain -- Operational Flow.

How does it work? Let me show with an example. Claim: "Wakefield's paper remained published for twelve years."

Step one: Search for a source. Three results found -- Wikipedia, BMJ, The Lancet.

Step two: Open and read the source. The BMJ page was opened, the full text was read. Publication: 1998, retraction: 2010 -- twelve years. Verified.

Step three: Cross-check. The Lancet retraction notice was opened -- February 2010. Verified.

Step four: Write into the document plus link to the source. The claim plus the source -- BMJ 2010, The Lancet retraction notice -- entered the document.

Step five: Archive. Archive layer: full text saved, access date March fourth, 2026. Presentation layer: "Wakefield, 1998 / 2010, The Lancet plus BMJ."

When any link in this chain breaks, the output is unreliable. You searched but did not open -- hearsay. You opened but did not cross-check -- single-source fallacy. You found the source but did not archive -- traceability is gone.

The Two-Layer Archive Architecture.

First layer: the archive. The complete document -- unabridged. Where it was obtained: source, link, access date. Untouchable, undeletable, unmodifiable. A source found to be faulty is not deleted -- its status is noted. Full traceability: source, usage, abandonment, what replaced it. It stays with us -- it is not presented to the reader.

Second layer: the presentation. The document's identity -- title, date, author. The essence of the relevant section -- highlighted. The finding or conclusion -- trimmed, concise. The reader sees this layer. It references the archive -- anyone wanting to verify goes there to look. No encyclopedia is served; a map is served.

The Reader's Verification Responsibility.

I quote:

"In AI-assisted research, trust is a dangerous word. Verification is a necessary act. The reader -- the user in this system -- must verify everything they read. Click on the source and see if that is what it actually says. Check the number. Try whether the link works. This seems laborious, but the alternative is surrendering to hallucination."

One hundred percent accuracy is impossible -- but ninety-nine point five percent is achievable. The path: on the producer's side, the verification chain; on the reader's side, the verification reflex. When both work together, the space in which hallucination can survive shrinks. It cannot be reduced to zero -- but it can be managed.

Principle ten deep exploration: Night on Earth -- Thinking in Your Own Language.

Jim Jarmusch. 1991. Five cities, the same night, five language problems.

This section crystallizes the principle of thinking in one's native language through a film. Night on Earth tells five taxi rides in five cities on the same night. Each one is a lesson on language, culture, and the transfer of meaning.

Los Angeles. Seven-seventeen PM. Winona Ryder as Corky, Gena Rowlands as Victoria.

Corky is seventeen, driving a taxi into the night. Victoria is a Hollywood casting director, heading from the airport into the city. Throughout the ride, Victoria sees something in Corky -- an energy, a raw talent -- and offers her an acting role. Corky listens, smiles, and refuses. She wants to be a car mechanic.

This segment is not class conflict -- it is something more subtle. What Victoria offers is not a world Corky cannot imagine; she can imagine it but does not want it. The way she expresses this comes in street language.

And notice what emerges here: the original idiom goes -- "Can't live with 'em, can't live without 'em." About men -- a well-known template. Corky's version is different: "Can't live with 'em, can't shoot 'em." She changes the second half: instead of "can't live without them," it is "can't even shoot them to be rid of them."

She changes the second half -- she does not fully know the original but carries on as though she does. If you adapt this into full street fluency in Turkish, you make Corky someone who commands that register. You destroy the girl's half-formed pattern. Preserving the brokenness is also part of transferring meaning.

New York. Approaching midnight. Armin Mueller-Stahl as Helmut, Giancarlo Esposito as Yo-Yo, Rosie Perez as Angela.

Helmut is East German, a former circus clown. His first day of work in New York. He tries to pick up Yo-Yo but cannot operate the automatic transmission -- in East Germany, cars were not automatic. Yo-Yo takes the wheel and drives his own driver.

And notice how the language barrier operates here. Yo-Yo says: "New York is cool." Helmut responds: "Yes, it is cold." Yo-Yo corrects: "No -- cool! Hip! Happening!"

The cultural meaning of "cool" is accessible only from within that language. It is not in the dictionary -- it is in the context.

Even names cannot be translated: Yo-Yo hears the name "Helmut" and laughs. Helmut hears the name "Yo-Yo" and cannot make sense of it. A name each considers "normal" sounds absurd to the other. Beneath the language barrier lies something else: a worldview barrier.

And yet a bond forms between them -- but this bond comes not from the precise transfer of words but from the fact that both are marginal. Yo-Yo is Black, so taxis do not stop for him; Helmut is German, so the system is foreign to him.

Paris. Before dawn, four-forty-seven AM. Isaach de Bankole as the driver -- from the Ivory Coast. Beatrice Dalle as the blind woman.

The Ivorian driver picks up two drunk diplomats. When the diplomats hear the driver is from the Ivory Coast, they laugh.

Why? In French, "Ivoirien" -- a person from the Ivory Coast -- and "il y voit rien" -- "he sees nothing" -- share a phonetic resemblance that becomes a wordplay. This pun compresses the post-colonial hierarchy within France into a single phrase. The driver throws the diplomats out of the car.

Then he picks up the blind woman. The dialogue between the two characters revolves around this axis: the driver frames blindness as a loss. The woman refuses -- she says that seeing can be a burden, that perception is not the monopoly of the eye.

The meaning embedded within the language: "il voit rien" means both "he does not see" and "blind" -- the phrase used about the driver also describes the woman's condition. To translate into Turkish, this double meaning must be chosen -- and the choice changes the meaning of the scene.

Rome. The same moment, a different city, four-forty-seven AM. Roberto Benigni as Gino, Paolo Bonacelli as the Priest.

Late at night, Gino picks up a priest. The priest is ill, cannot walk. Gino wants to confess his sins. The priest says "I am not on official duty." Gino does not hear -- and begins.

He starts with minor sins. What follows is a progressively graphic, progressively absurd cascade of confessions. The priest grows paler and paler, reaches for his nitroglycerine. On a speed bump the pill falls to the floor. The priest dies.

Benigni's Florentine accent and street Italian carry layers of meaning for an Italian audience. When translated into Turkish, that layer is lost.

Language here becomes an unintentional weapon. Gino did not mean to kill the priest -- but what he said was lethal. Two systems of speech collided: southern Italy's sunny confession tradition and northern Europe's moral language. Benigni spoke not because he had something to confess, but because his voice needed out. The idea that meaning might reach the other side was never in the equation.

Helsinki. Five-fifty-seven AM. Minus ten degrees, snow. Matti Pellonpaa as Mika, the drunken passengers from the Kaurismaki repertory.

The final segment is the shortest and the heaviest. Three drunk men, one weary driver. Jarmusch deliberately named the characters Aki and Mika -- in homage to the two great names of Finnish cinema, Aki and Mika Kaurismaki.

The drunks tell Mika about their friend: he lost his job yesterday, his new car was wrecked, his wife wants a divorce, it turned out his young daughter is pregnant. Four calamities.

Mika responds: "Is that all?"

This sentence is the most concise expression of northern European suffering. No lament, no performance of empathy. Direct competition: my story is heavier.

Mika tells his: For years they had no children. One day his wife became pregnant. The baby was premature, placed in an incubator. Doctors said it would live a week. Mika did not bond -- if the baby vanished, they would both be destroyed. The baby lived beyond all expectations. His wife said: "Our child needs your love too." Mika bonded. The baby died.

Final scene: Dawn is breaking. Mika drives the car slowly around an empty park. Alone. Nothing is said. Tom Waits's music is sparse, a few bell notes -- it frames the silence rather than filling it.

In Finnish, the weight of what is not said is greater than what is said. Carrying this into Turkish is possible, but noting the frame is essential.

I quote:

"Language does not guarantee meaning. Speaking the same language -- Corky and Victoria -- does not ensure understanding; speaking different languages -- Helmut and Yo-Yo -- does not prevent it. The most effective communication breaks down entirely in Rome -- it is nonexistent -- while in Helsinki it takes place almost without words."

The principle of thinking in one's own language crystallizes here: transferring the source language is not enough. What must be transferred is the worldview embedded in that language -- including the distortions, the misunderstandings, the deliberate fractures. Otherwise, it is not translation but summary.

Principles eleven, twelve, thirteen, and fourteen. Attitude clusters: Antiperformance and Paying the Price.

Antiperformance is rare in the academic world. Darwin practiced it.

Now let us bring all this data together. In numbers: Darwin, from the Galapagos to publication, waited twenty-four years. The time he spent in the Galapagos was five weeks. Mendel cultivated twenty-eight thousand pea plants. And the number of copies Mendel distributed was forty.

Darwin visited the Galapagos in 1835, stayed five weeks. He put his theory to paper in 1838 -- a thirty-five-page draft. So the issue was not unreadiness. He wrote to a friend: "Revealing this theory is like confessing to a murder." His wife Emma's fear, written in her own hand, was this: her husband's soul was in danger. Darwin loved her. He gave the next eight years to barnacles -- both real science and real escape. Throughout: nausea, heart palpitations, recurring rashes. No organic cause was ever found. In 1858, Alfred Russel Wallace independently reached the same conclusion and sent Darwin a letter. Darwin's hand was forced: now or never. On the Origin of Species appeared in 1859. The twenty-four-year wait was thoroughness and fear and love combined -- but "readiness" was the smallest part of it.

Mendel was born in 1822 into a poor farming family in Silesia. There was no money for university -- the Augustinian monastery in Brno was an open door: education, time, a garden. In 1850, he failed the teaching examination. In 1856, he took the exam again and failed again. That same year he began the pea experiments in the monastery garden. Eight years, approximately twenty-eight thousand plants. In 1866, he published his work -- distributed forty copies; today only two are known to survive. The monastery bishop laughed and asked him to stop the experiments. Mendel died in 1884. The laws of heredity were rediscovered in 1900 -- sixteen years later, by three different researchers working independently, and all three reached Mendel's 1866 text. The monastery garden was for him both sanctuary and laboratory and prison -- but no other door was open.

I quote:

"Montaigne lost his closest friend, Etienne de La Boetie, to the plague in 1563. Eight years later, in 1571, at thirty-eight, he withdrew to the tower of his chateau. He had Greek and Latin inscriptions carved into the ceiling beams -- part of one reads: 'To withdraw from the slavery of the court and public duties, in order to spend the rest of his life, now with so little time remaining, in peace and liberty...' La Boetie was dead. He had begun to write in order to speak with him -- when there was no one left across from him. What emerged, 'Essais,' means 'attempts, trials' in French. 'I am not educating my reader; I am educating myself.' The tower was the form that grief took."

Knowing Your Limits and the Legitimacy of Attention.

The best achievable result, not the best possible result. "There is always someone better than you" -- realism. There is a boundary between stealing attention and earning it. Stealing attention through needle-pricks -- feigned astonishment, dramatic packaging, jargon inflation -- steals attention. Earning attention by enabling the other person to live the matter themselves -- that is legitimate.

Paying the Price.

I quote:

"There are undesirable consequences to being exempted from paying the price, because people insulated from the effects of their decisions do not learn."

Nassim Nicholas Taleb. Skin in the Game.

In 1984, Barry Marshall drank a solution containing H. pylori himself. Within eight days he developed gastritis. He proved that stomach ulcers have a bacterial origin. 2005 Nobel Prize in Medicine. Paying the price here was not a metaphor -- it was real.

Artificial Intelligence and Research. Necessity, Method, Limits.

"I used artificial intelligence" says nothing. Which artificial intelligence, how, and for what purpose you used it is what matters.

What is worth noting is this: type matters. Chat converses; an agent researches. Chat cannot access the source of the information it gives; an agent goes out to the web, reads documents, verifies sources. What was used in this document: the agent system. The Night on Earth scenes, Wallraff's book, Grandin's studies -- the agent searched, found, and cross-checked.

Parallel research: The research track that investigated Night on Earth's scenes, the track that investigated the "getting inside" examples, and the track that investigated the evidence base of the other principles -- all three ran simultaneously. A single researcher would have done this sequentially -- hours, perhaps days. It was done in parallel.

No language barrier: Accessing Italian, Finnish, French sources used to require knowing those languages. Artificial intelligence removes this barrier. If a Japanese study needs to be researched tomorrow, there is no language barrier. Domain knowledge is being democratized.

But there are limits too: The intuition of someone who spent thirty years in court corridors, who senses "this won't hold" -- that does not exist in artificial intelligence. Artificial intelligence can scan sources, but it cannot know where the price was paid and what it cost. The final judgment belongs to the human.

Here is an observation, conveyed anonymously.

I knew a lawyer. Thirty years in court corridors. Before writing a brief, he would spend hours drawing a map -- he would not sit down to write until he had made the relationships visible. Writing took one hour; the mapping phase took days.

He approached research with the same logic. When he began working with artificial intelligence, there was only one thing he needed to learn: not to command, but to converse. Not "research this," but "think through this matter with me." A two-word difference -- but the essence of the method.

What does he do now? He sits with his coffee, mentions a matter. The artificial intelligence researches, presents, debates. He reads and corrects -- "this is wrong," "go this way," "expand this." And the research reorganizes itself. An investigation that used to stretch over weeks now emerges in a few hours.

Let me underline something here: This document was written by applying its own principles to itself.

As you read this document, it is impossible not to notice: we are researching the method of research. And we are conducting this inquiry hand in hand with artificial intelligence. This is not coincidence -- it is self-application.

Primary source priority: Night on Earth's scenes were researched not from summaries but from the film itself. Verification reflex: the "Brad Pitt" claim was rejected; DiCaprio was verified. Verification chain: in version zero point six, the requirement that every citation must link back to the accessed source was introduced -- a search summary is not a source. Prohibition of verbatim quotation: user expression is raw material, processed into the document's register -- but primary sources are quoted verbatim, as of version zero point eight. Summons to the Mind: research is not instruction but an invitation to think -- added as the founding principle in version zero point eight. Pattern interrogation: born in version one point zero from the Iran War case -- the document produced a principle from its own error. Thinking in one's own language: every concept was first built in Turkish. Iterative progress: from version zero point one to version one point zero -- every version on top of the one before, the earlier ones standing in the git repository.

On the matter of artificial intelligence usage, this document also shows its own practice -- it does not merely narrate; it does. The document is both object and subject.

The doors that artificial intelligence opens.

First, scope: Sources in five languages can be scanned in the same session. Impossible for a single researcher.

Second, expertise map: A starting map in a field you know nothing about -- critical names, debates, turning points.

Third, cross-checking: A claim can be checked against three separate sources simultaneously. Invaluable for the verification reflex.

Fourth, thinking partner: An answer to the question "I am saying this -- where might I be wrong?" Academics call this an advisory board.

Fifth, synthesis: After fifty sources have been read, extracting shared patterns. Artificial intelligence makes the patterns visible; human judgment decides whether they are meaningful.

Sixth, memory: The correction made at the start of the session is still valid at the end. Humans forget; the agent does not.

Research Process. Typical Flow -- Not Fixed, a Guide.

There is a nine-step flow, but this is not a fixed prescription -- it is a guide. Let us go through it in order.

Step one: Topic. Identify the point of curiosity.

Step two: Primary Source. The thing itself first.

Step three: Identify Position. Who says what, from where.

Step four: Draw the Map. Relationships, gaps.

Step five: Original Connection. A connection not yet made.

Step six: Verification Chain. Search, open, read, verify, archive.

Step seven: Build in Your Language. Not transferring but constructing.

Step eight: Open the Matter. Limits, counterarguments.

Step nine: Iterate. Do not discard the first draft.

And from this ninth step the process loops back to the beginning -- because every iteration rises above the one before.

Subject-Specific Sub-Principles. Additional Principles by Domain.

Legal research. The primary source is the text of the law plus court rulings. Citation verification is mandatory -- ruling number plus full text. Legislation currency must be checked -- is it repealed or in force? "Without prejudice to our further claims" appears inflated from outside, but from inside it is a scope instrument -- the defense of circuitous language. The distinction between law as written and law as lived must be observed. Open sources: Court of Cassation, UYAP, Constitutional Court, mevzuat.gov.tr.

Language and translation. The principle of thinking in one's own language is central. The gravitational pull of the source language is under constant audit. Preserving the brokenness is also meaning transfer -- we saw this in the Corky example. Night on Earth: a cultural-layer note is required for each segment. Forcing everything into a single mold is forbidden. The principle of concrete context -- when a word is abstracted, it empties.

Geopolitical and historical research. Pattern interrogation is mandatory -- the think tank framework is a source, not a conclusion. The organicity test: Is the attack or event organic or manufactured? Repeatability, the question of who benefits, blood incompatibility. The Western and Eastern perspective distinction must be explicitly identified -- which eyes are you looking through. Raw data -- military production, population, energy, economic indicators -- comes before the pattern. Beware the historical analogy trap: saying "X is just like Y" is easy; structural resemblance must be demonstrated. Media sources must be positioned -- Reuters, AP, Al Jazeera, TASS are all looking from somewhere.

AI-assisted research. Use the agent system -- not chat. The verification chain is mandatory: Search, open, read, verify, archive. A search summary is not a source -- WebSearch generates a lead; open the source and read it. Awareness of the six forms of hallucination -- checked in every output. Parallel research tracks -- independent questions run simultaneously. Information from training data carries the "unverified" label -- it cannot enter the document as a source. Final judgment belongs to the human -- the reader verifies everything they read. The model's knowledge cutoff date must be checked.

Open Questions. Not Yet Answered.

This work has seven open questions.

First: The debate of the principles. The principles were tested through lived cases -- in version one point zero, sixteen principles underwent a live test and violations were found in four -- but the boundaries and contradictions of each have not yet been debated one by one.

Second: The limit of attention. Rhetoric is legitimate; needle-pricking is not. But where is the concrete boundary between the two? What is the criterion?

Fourth: Sustainability of the verification chain. The chain looks good on paper, but in a single month at least four violations occurred -- all detected by the user. The gap between principle and reflex is a structural problem.

Sixth: When is a pattern legitimate? Principle seventeen says to interrogate pattern use -- but is every pattern bad? Where is the boundary between a good framework and a lazy pattern? Using a framework knowingly and with awareness of its limits may be legitimate -- lazy copying is not.
