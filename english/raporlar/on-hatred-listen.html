On Hatred

"The anatomy of an emotion."

On Hatred. The anatomy of an emotion. Halit Cengiz Uzuner, the twenty-second of March, two thousand twenty-six.

I am Yontu. What you are about to hear is the dismantling report of an emotion. Hatred — a structure that cannot be justified by reason, that is not built by the intellect, that serves no function for the living. The author sets out to take this structure apart. Starting from a hatred he experienced the day before, he examines that hatred's object, its logic, its origins, its relationship to punishment, its bond with helplessness, and its difference from organic evil.

The text consists of seven sections. The first opens the problem of the object — hatred demands an object, but the object never fits. The second discusses punishment — even the law does not say "destroy." The third asks whether hatred belongs to the human — a pre-rational reflex, yet a mechanism that cannot function without reason. The fourth examines the violation of expectation — no expectation, no hatred. The fifth lays bare the confession of surrendered will — to hate is to have handed over your own agency. The sixth introduces the concept of organic evil — false goodness is more worthless than organic evil. And the seventh draws the territory that must not be abandoned — neither to heaven-and-hell economics nor to neuron maps.

Between the sections there are paintings — Goya, Munch, Caravaggio, Bosch, Blake. Each one a visual thought accompanying the argument. I will describe those too.

Let us begin.

On the cover of the text is Goya's famous painting — Saturn Devouring His Son. Painted between eighteen nineteen and eighteen twenty-three, now in the Prado Museum. Against a dark background, a giant with maddened eyes, eating his own child. Perhaps hatred is something like this — devouring what it has produced.

Yesterday I hated someone. A state. The State of Israel. They are killing children, bombing hospitals, annihilating an entire people before the eyes of humanity — and there is nothing I can do. I hated. I made extravagant outbursts. I delivered absolute judgments. Then night came, morning came, and today I turned my gaze upon the hatred itself.

The moment you try to defend the claim that you hate someone — if you possess an analytical mind — that hatred collapses. You start arranging your reasons in a logical sequence. Unless your emotions intervene from the background, that sequence cannot be assembled. You triggered hatred with a chain of reasoning that never actually formed. And you look at the object of your hatred: does this object fit the hatred? You cannot find a fit.

This essay was born from that moment of not finding.

Here in the text appears Edvard Munch's The Scream — eighteen ninety-three, in the Nasjonalmuseet in Oslo. Beneath a sky that seems to ripple, a figure clutching its face with both hands, mouth agape. Munch had written, "I heard the infinite scream of nature." And the text asks: is hatred, too, such a scream — or is it a false name given to a scream?

Section One. The Problem of the Object. Hatred demands an object. But the object never fits.

When I say "I hate the State of Israel," what have I actually said? The State of Israel: millions of people, thousands of years of history, hundreds of distinct currents of thought, a state apparatus, an army, civilians, dissidents, war protesters marching in the streets — all compressed into a single object. An analytical mind cannot be satisfied by that generalization. Generalization is the necessary precondition of hatred — but it is the fatal error of analysis.

Then the object shifts. What I actually hate is not the perpetrator but the act. I hate the killing of children in Gaza. When I look at that act, the hatred stands firm — because the act is concrete, definable, bounded. But the feeling of hatred does not stay with the act; it spreads to the perpetrator. This spread is automatic, reflexive, happens in milliseconds. "Who did this?" — that is the first movement of the emotion. And the moment that question is asked, the act recedes into the background, the perpetrator steps forward. A state, a nation, a religion — the generalization is complete.

Hatred generalizes. Anger focuses. Perhaps what I experienced yesterday was not hatred — it was anger. But the moment I gave anger the name "hatred," it spread from the act to the perpetrator, became boundless, became uncontrollable.

"If it were built by reason, we could verify it by reason. We cannot."

Section Two. Punishment Is Not the Vomiting of Hatred. Even the law — humanity's most institutional attempt at justice — does not say "destroy."

Here in the text appears a painting by Caravaggio — Judith Beheading Holofernes. Painted between fifteen ninety-eight and fifteen ninety-nine, in the Palazzo Barberini in Rome. Judith is cutting Holofernes' neck with a sword. But what draws the eye is this: there is no hatred on Judith's face — there is resolve. The act is terrible, but the motivation is not vengeance. It is protection.

The repetition of an act begins to define the perpetrator. Systematic, deliberate, repeated acts — yes, these reveal a structure. And that structure should be punished. But the purpose of punishment is not to vomit hatred.

The purpose of punishment is to rehabilitate the offender. To protect others. To maintain social order. You do not punish someone because you hate them — you punish them to protect others from them and to rehabilitate them. You cannot rehabilitate by destroying. You certainly cannot by hating. You contain them — that is all.

And even in that act of containment, the causal link you draw between offender and consequence is never a complete cause-and-effect chain. You cannot establish a scientific link. As a lawyer I have lived this for thirty years: adequate causation, foreseeability, intervening factors, conditions — all approximations. None certainty. You construct the nearest approximation for that day's circumstances, and even that is only that day's answer. Yesterday's justice is not tomorrow's justice.

If justice is a function of time — then hatred, which freezes time, ignores conditions, and demands the absolute — is structurally incompatible with reality.

Section Three. Does Hatred Belong to the Human? A pre-rational reflex. But a mechanism that cannot function without reason.

Here in the text appears the Hell Panel of Hieronymus Bosch's The Garden of Earthly Delights — painted between fourteen ninety and fifteen ten, in the Prado Museum. A dark hellscape — people being tortured, filled with fantastical creatures, a world where all order has collapsed. But the text's note is this: Bosch's hell is not hatred — it is the oldest visual map of hatred's instrumentalization. The heaven-and-hell economy: feed hatred with fear, reward fear with obedience.

It is difficult even to discuss hatred in terms of meaning. Its definition is troubled. Does hatred belong to the human? Is it an extension of being alive?

An animal can feel fear — survival of the organism, the amygdala, fight-or-flight. An animal can feel anger — defense of territory, aggression. These are bodily, momentary, situational. When the threat passes, they subside. But an animal does not hate.

Hatred requires the following: object identification — "this thing"; persistence — "always"; generalization — "all of them"; remembering — "they did this before too"; thinking about it again and again. All of these are cognitive operations. But they are not rational operations.

Here is the paradox: hatred requires reason but does not originate from reason. Fear comes from the body; the brain processes it. Anger comes from the body; the brain directs it. But hatred? Hatred takes the raw material that comes from the body — fear, pain, helplessness, perceived threat — and clothes it in structure. It fixes it to an object, stretches it across time, generates justification. This structuring is automatic, reflexive — but it cannot function without a cognitive substrate.

Hatred is neither pure emotion nor pure thought. It is a counterfeit compound of the two.

Let me underline something here. The Helplessness Transformation. What is the raw material of hatred? Fear and helplessness. You look at the children in Gaza — you feel pain, you feel helpless, there is nothing you can do. Helplessness is the most unbearable of emotions. And hatred transforms helplessness: instead of "I cannot do anything," it says "I hate." Hatred gives helplessness the sensation of power. A false power — it changes nothing — but more bearable than helplessness. In that sense, yes, it is a psychological defense mechanism. But a defense against what? Against your own impotence.

"Hatred is emphatically not a concept constructed by reason. If it were, we could verify it by reason."
Here in the text appears another painting by Goya — The Third of May Eighteen Oh Eight. Painted in eighteen fourteen, in the Prado Museum. A line of soldiers firing upon assembled civilians. In the center, a man in a white shirt, arms flung wide — on his face not hatred but raw terror and helplessness. The hatred is outside this painting — it is in the viewer. Goya is the painter who painted the viewer's emotion without placing it inside the canvas.

Section Four. The Violation of Expectation. Hatred is born from the judgment "you should not have been this way." No expectation, no hatred.

You feel hatred toward Israel because you expect humane behavior from a state. Your expectation is violated; hatred is born. You assume a world order: states should not kill civilians, children should be protected, hospitals should be inviolable. When that expectation breaks, the judgment "this should not have happened" forms — and that judgment is the fuel of hatred.

Now consider: Anton Chigurh. A footnote here — Chigurh is the character from the Coen Brothers' film No Country for Old Men. Adapted from Cormac McCarthy's novel of the same name. A serial killer who murders at random, determines fate by a coin toss, with zero empathy capacity — yet whose internal logic is perfectly consistent.

A character who illustrates the pure nature of evil to perfection. You cannot execute that man, you cannot destroy him — and you cannot even hate him.

Why? Because you expect nothing from Chigurh. He shows you what he is from the first moment. Nothing hidden, nothing false, no pretense of being something else. He flips his coin, calls it fate, and his internal logic is entirely consistent. No expectation, no disappointment; no disappointment, no hatred.

Hatred feeds on the perception of falseness. "You should not have been this way, yet you are" — that contradiction is the fuel of hatred. In Chigurh there is no contradiction. He is what he is. Organic evil carries no pretense — and a human being cannot hate what carries no pretense.

Let us pause here for a moment. The Chigurh Test — Theories of Punishment.

Retribution: Collapses. No hatred, no motive for vengeance.

Rehabilitation: Collapses. There is nothing to change. He is not broken — he is different.

Deterrence: Collapses. He has his own rationality; it does not intersect with the rationality of the penal system.

Only one thing remains: incapacitation. "We cannot change you, we are not avenging ourselves on you, we cannot deter you — but we can protect others from you." The most primitive yet most honest form of punishment. And it has no relationship whatsoever with hatred.

Here in the text, two paintings appear side by side. On the left, Caravaggio's David with the Head of Goliath — painted between sixteen oh nine and sixteen ten. David holds Goliath's severed head. On the right, Goya's Duel with Cudgels — painted between eighteen twenty and eighteen twenty-three. Two men sunk to their knees in mud, beating each other with clubs. Both are in the swamp, and both are sinking.

Section Five. The Confession of Surrendered Will. To hate is to have handed over your own agency.

For a long time I had stopped hating people whose malice I had witnessed, or who I believed had in some way contributed to events that hurt me. I had forgiven them, inwardly. But this was not an emotional forgiveness — it was an analytical one.

Because I had said to myself: your actions contributed, but they did not directly cause the outcome. I allowed you to do these things — directly or indirectly. Your actions alone were not sufficient to produce this result.

And here is the breaking point: if their actions had been directly sufficient — if they could have done it on their own — that would mean my will had stopped nothing, that I was entirely useless. To hate them would be, indirectly, to see myself as worthless.

This is the most hidden face of hatred. When you hate someone, you are telling them: "You did this alone. I could do nothing." That is the acceptance of helplessness. The one who hates is impotent — because they are saying "they acted, I was powerless."

My position is different: "I was there too. I also did or did not do something — the responsibility is shared." This is the cost of possessing a will. But also its reward. To hate erases both our shares. To forgive — analytically, by accepting responsibility — acknowledges the existence of both of us.

"Hatred is incompatible with the possession of will. To hate is to have surrendered it."
Here in the text appears William Blake's The Great Red Dragon and the Woman Clothed with the Sun — painted between eighteen oh five and eighteen ten, in the Brooklyn Museum. An immense dragon with wings spread wide, beneath it a defenseless woman. But the text's note is this: Blake's dragon is not an object of hatred but an object of examination. The visual archetype of organic evil — faithful to its nature, nothing hidden, nothing false.

Section Six. Organic Evil. False goodness is more worthless than organic evil.

Love is troubled too. Love, too, cannot be verified by reason. If you tried to arrange your reasons for loving someone in a logical sequence, the same collapse would occur. So what is the difference between love and hatred?

Perhaps verifiability is not the only criterion. Perhaps both are productions. Both pre-rational, both troubled — but both putting something into the world. Love can be an art. The harmless form of hatred might be one as well.

I enjoy evil sometimes. Provided it is deeply organic, provided its nature is worth examining.

That sentence carries the core of my worldview. I reject falseness, not evil. Calculated manipulation, engineered cruelty, false goodness — these disgust me. But an evil faithful to its own nature? That can be examined. Because its structure is visible, not hidden.

This is the fundamental criterion: organic, or false? False goodness is more worthless than organic evil. False love is more hollow than organic hatred. Because in the false there is nothing to examine — in the organic there is structure, there is nature, there is something that can be taken apart.

Yesterday's hatred was a hatred fixed to its object, surrendered to emotion, its structure unquestioned. Today I took the same hatred and disassembled it, extracted its structure, stress-tested its chain of logic, interrogated its object, investigated its relationship to being alive. The hatred is the same hatred — but yesterday it owned me; today it is my material.

Section Seven. The Territory That Must Not Be Abandoned. Neither to the heaven-and-hell economy nor to neuron maps.

Hatred is trapped between two forms of reductionism.

The first is religious reductionism. The heaven-and-hell economy: "Hate, so that you may earn heaven" or "Do not hate, so that you may escape hell." Both turn hatred into an instrument of trade. Cults use hatred as a mechanism of allegiance: hating a shared enemy generates belonging to the group. Excommunication, takfir, expulsion — all institutional forms of hatred. Hatred here is the tool; the purpose is control.

The second is materialist reductionism. "Hatred is amygdala activation, cortisol elevation, serotonin depletion." This too is not hatred — it is the shadow of hatred. Measuring chemistry is not understanding emotion. Scanning the brain is not reading the mind. You cannot see hatred on an MRI — you see the bodily trace of hatred. The trace and the thing are not the same.

This territory — the nature, structure, and function of hatred — must not be surrendered to religions and belief systems. They exploit this emotion within heaven-and-hell economies, within cult-like structures. But to examine it solely through neurons and chemical processes is also a kind of stupidity. Both are forms of escape: one into dogma, the other into data.

There is a third path: to examine hatred as a human experience, with its own structure, its own logic, its own mode of collapse. Neither saying "hatred is a sin" nor "hatred is a neurochemical reaction." Hatred cannot be verified by reason; it does not fit its object; it requires the violation of expectation; it is the confession of surrendered will — these are sentences that belong neither to a preacher nor to a neuroscientist. They are the sentences of a person who has faced hatred itself.

Here in the text, Goya's cover painting returns — Saturn Devouring His Son. But this time the text's note is different: hatred, like Saturn, devours what it has produced. But when Goya painted this painting — when he turned hatred into an object of examination — he broke Saturn's power.

Fear is organic — it serves the preservation of life. Anger is organic — it serves the defense of boundaries. Love is organic — it serves the formation of bonds. Hatred? The only pre-rational emotion that does not serve being alive. Perhaps it is not even an emotion.

Perhaps a malfunction. Perhaps a structuring error. Perhaps the cognitive structuring of bodily, emotional responses — fear, pain, helplessness — but without any rational foundation. Dressing raw emotion in the costume of logic. A counterfeit rationalization.

But even as a malfunction, so long as it is organic, it is worth examining. Yesterday hatred owned me. Today it is my material. Tomorrow it may become a dismantling tool in someone else's hands.

Here is the painful part: if you taught this in a criminology class, most of the students would still go on hating. Only a few people who use their minds — people who can place a gap between feeling and judgment — would not. Legal education is precisely the place that should teach that gap. And even there, most fail.

But a few succeed. And this essay is for them.
