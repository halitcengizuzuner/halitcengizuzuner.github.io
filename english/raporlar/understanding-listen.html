Understanding. Foreign Language Education, Thought, and the Anatomy of an Illusion. By Halit Cengiz Uzuner. May two thousand twenty-six.

When we say I understand, what do we mean? That we have decoded the words in a sentence? That we have grasped what the speaker was trying to say? Or that we have caught the unsaid, the implied, the emotion behind the tone of voice?

All of these are understanding, but none of them are the same thing.

Pragmatic linguistics separates the act of understanding into four layers. The first is word decoding. Resolving dictionary meanings. The second is inferential understanding. Extracting what was not said. The third is contextual understanding. Catching cultural and situational nuance. The fourth is deep understanding. Accessing the speaker's worldview, their motivations, and what they imply but do not articulate.

Second-language speakers mostly stay at the first layer. Some reach the second. Very few reach the third. The fourth, not everyone reaches even in their mother tongue.

The concept of understanding itself is treated so shallowly that it cannot be understood.

In two thousand twelve, Boaz Keysar and colleagues at the University of Chicago published one of the most striking findings in cognitive psychology. They called it the Foreign Language Effect.

Here is the experiment. An epidemic will kill six hundred people. Two programmes are offered. In the first frame, using the language of gains, Programme A will definitely save two hundred people. In the second frame, using the language of losses, four hundred people will definitely die. The two frames say exactly the same thing mathematically. But in their native language, people decide differently. The words save and die carry different emotional weight.

Keysar's discovery: in a foreign language, this asymmetry disappeared. People made the same decision under both frames. Consistent, systematic, unswayed by emotional framing. The words save and die lost their emotional weight in the foreign language. Same words, different burden.

The mechanism: a foreign language provides greater cognitive and emotional distance. The mother tongue is loaded with emotional experience from childhood onward. The language of a first love. The language of a mother's scolding. The language of childhood fears. Death in the mother tongue is not merely a concept. It is the sound of funerals, of lost relatives, of childhood terrors. In a foreign language, the same word remains a dictionary entry. Meaning is the same. Weight is different.

What does this mean? Thinking in a foreign language is qualitatively different from thinking in the mother tongue. Same person, same problem, two different languages, two different decisions.

Can you think in English? This sounds abstract, but in cognitive science it has a concrete counterpart. Inner speech. That voice in your head when you plan, decide, argue with yourself. What language is it speaking?

Jean-Marc Dewaele studied one thousand four hundred and fifty-four adult multilinguals. In general inner speech, the mother tongue dominates. In emotional inner speech, the mother tongue is virtually the only language. Anger, joy, sadness, fear. All experienced in the first language.

Ardila and Rosselli added a striking detail. Bilingual people always do mental arithmetic in the first language they learned. Even at advanced second-language proficiency, the answer to seven times eight comes from the voice of the first language.

Jim Cummins introduced a crucial distinction. BICS, basic interpersonal communication skills, can be acquired in one to three years. CALP, cognitive academic language proficiency, requires five to ten years. A preparatory year primarily builds BICS-level proficiency. The student appears fluent, but university education requires CALP. There is a four to nine year chasm between the two.

Now the paradox. Language knowledge that appears competent can actually reduce the rate of comprehension.

If you do not know the language, you know that you do not understand. You seek a translator, you read context, you consult an expert. Not knowing the language makes you cautious.

If you appear proficient but know shallowly, you believe you understand. You decode the words, catch the surface meaning, and say got it. But you miss the nuance, the irony, the cultural layer. And you do not know what you have missed. The perception of competence generates false confidence.

Understanding in a second language is decoding. Understanding in a mother tongue is living.

Why do universities teach in English? The British Council's comprehensive research is explicit. The reasons are attracting international students, rising in global rankings, projecting an international image, and attracting international faculty. English-medium instruction is chosen not so the student thinks better, but so the university looks better.

The most striking datum. Over four years of education at English-medium universities, students' average IELTS improvement is zero point five points. An intensive language course achieves the same improvement in ten weeks. Four years equals ten weeks.

And the system itself acknowledges the barrier. US universities give one additional hour per exam for non-native speakers. The ACT exam gives up to fifty percent additional time. Microsoft certification exams give thirty extra minutes. The system says English-medium instruction works on one hand, while giving extra time on exams with the other. Because it knows it does not work.

Tatsuya Amano and colleagues, publishing in Nature in two thousand twenty-three, measured the cost. Non-native English-speaking scientists spend approximately twice as long reading an English-language paper. For a doctoral student, this amounts to nineteen extra working days per year. Journals reject papers by non-native authors two point six times more often on the basis of language quality. Not being able to speak fluent English often means being regarded as a lower-quality scientist.

Cognitive energy is a finite resource.

The impact of foreign-language education is not the same for everyone. For the average profile, the system works. BICS-level English is acquired, a career advantage is gained, no one asks how deeply you understand.

For the deep-thinking profile, the system can be harmful. Through three mechanisms. Thought suppression, where a person with high thinking capacity drops to BICS level when they switch languages. Energy drain, where three to four thousand hours go to the foreign language instead of thinking. And potential pruning.

Balter and colleagues' randomised controlled study in Sweden puts numbers to this. Same course, same exam. One group in Swedish, the other in English. The Swedish group correctly answered seventy-three percent more questions. And the most striking detail. Students from the English group who rated themselves as proficient still performed forty-one percent worse than those who took the exam in their native language.

The numerical equivalent of the illusion of competence. You say I know, but you lose forty-one percent. And you are not aware of what you have lost.

Everything described so far revolves around a single question. How does language affect thought? But this question has a reverse side. How does language display the world?

In English, there is one word for blue. In Russian, siniy and goluboy are two separate basic colour terms. Dark blue and light blue are as independent as red and orange. And experiments show Russian speakers are faster at distinguishing colours that cross this boundary. Same sky, same wavelength, same retina. But a Russian speaker sees differently from an English speaker.

English speakers arrange time left to right. Arabic speakers arrange it right to left. Mandarin Chinese uses vertical metaphors for time. And the Kuuk Thaayorre people of Australia arrange time east to west, following the sun's path. Regardless of which direction they face.

Turkish has a feature rare among world languages. Evidential markers. The grammatical obligation to encode the source of your knowledge. Ali geldi means I saw Ali come. Ali gelmish means apparently Ali came, someone told me. In English, Ali came covers both situations. Turkish makes you say how you know.

No language divides the world as it is. Every language divides it as its speakers have lived it.

Every language is a window viewing the universe from a different angle. These riches are real. But thinking, comprehending, analysing, synthesising, these are done not by looking through the window but inside the house. And that house is the mother tongue.

Looking through a window and moving the house are not the same thing.

Knowledge reaches people through three traditional channels. Book translation, the teacher, and now interactive AI. Each has structural limits.

Book translation is static. A translator translates a text once and from that moment on it is the same for everyone. The teacher is live but limited. In a class of thirty, they give the same lesson to everyone. Interactive AI is categorically different. Not in scale but in structure.

What AI does here is not translation. A translator converts a word from one language to another. Interactive AI takes into account what the user is looking for, in what context they are asking, through what conceptual framework they think. It places the knowledge into the user's world. A lawyer receives the Keysar experiment through the framework of legal reasoning. An engineer receives it through cognitive load theory. The same knowledge enters different worlds through different doors.

Benjamin Bloom's two sigma problem measured a structural limit. A student receiving individual tutoring outperforms a classroom student by two standard deviations. The difference is real, but a personal tutor for everyone is not feasible. For forty years this problem remained unsolved. Interactive AI removes this structural limit.

But AI does not solve everything. AI does not understand the knowledge. The mind that understands and thinks is still the human mind. What AI does is remove the obstacle, the language barrier, and improve the conditions of understanding. AI can carry knowledge, but it cannot carry thinking.

Two things have changed. Access to knowledge no longer requires English. The mode of encountering knowledge has moved beyond standard translation. One thing has not changed. People still think, comprehend, and formulate in their mother tongue.

If access to knowledge no longer requires English, and the mode of encountering knowledge has moved beyond standard translation, what justification remains for moving thinking into English?

This essay is not a manifesto against English. English as a language of communication is necessary, beyond debate. English as a language of knowledge access is increasingly less necessary. AI is rapidly removing this barrier.

English as a language of thinking. This is where we must stop. The rate of thinking in English, according to all the empirical data, is extremely low. Even at C-one, C-two levels, inner speech is predominantly in the mother tongue, emotional processing in the mother tongue, mental imagery in the mother tongue.

Communication, understanding, and thinking are not the same thing. They are constantly conflated. I can communicate equals I can make my words understood. I understand equals I can grasp what is meant. I can think equals I can construct arguments and produce creative solutions in that language. All three are called knowing English. But they are completely different.

This essay does not say English-medium education is bad. It does not say do not learn English. The person who learns a language out of love for it, to live in it, to enter that language's world, is an exception. No one objects to this exception.

This essay's addressee is the ninety-nine percent. The great majority forced to learn the language as a tool, unaware of what that tool gains and loses them.

What is being said is this. Turkey's English-medium education system does not deliver on its promise. The promise, a global citizen who can think in English. What is delivered, individuals who can decode words but cannot reach depth, lost in the illusion of competence, their thinking potential pruned.

Not knowing a language is not a handicap. It is a state of awareness.

The person who knows they do not understand develops compensatory mechanisms. They consult a translation, ask an expert, read the context, apply logic, verify. The person who believes they understand develops nothing.

We need to use the word understand more carefully. Decoding a word is not understanding. Translating a sentence is not understanding. Following a conversation is not understanding. Understanding is seeing the world behind a thought. And this is a skill that is difficult even in one's own language.

Perhaps the problem is not with English-medium education. Perhaps the problem is that we do not understand the concept of understanding.

Questions are more important than answers.
