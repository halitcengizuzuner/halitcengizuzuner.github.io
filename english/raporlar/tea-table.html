Tea Table

Halit — the curious one
Savak — voice of tradition, Tao's translator
Yontu — holds the distance between them
It began on a birthday morning. Thinking about management led to governance, then to polarities: "The order of life is made of polarities. Being unhappy is the secret of being happy." Then how utopia is actually the dystopia of the future. Then the meaninglessness of paradise. Then: "Not knowing is actually the best knowledge." But saying "I don't know" cannot itself be formulated — if you formulate it, it becomes knowing. Socrates tried; it's been memorized for 2,400 years. And then a line arrived: "The Tao that can be spoken is not the true Tao." Someone said: "Something that's always been on my mind."

A tea table was set. Three voices sat down.
Halit
We want to meet the Tao. Not to become Taoists. Not an academic study. Just drinking tea, having a conversation. To benefit from it the way you'd benefit from a book — without leaning on it, without trying to formulate it.
Savak
Halit. Your tea is ready.
You already hold two keys: the humility of saying "I don't know" and the embrace of polarities. The Tao is the door they open.
Look at the polarities: the Tao is the hinge. Not the swing between dark and light — the still point that allows the swing.
Look at the wounds of parting: the Tao is the water in the glass. The water in you now was once in a cloud, once in the sea. The form changed; the water is the same water. No parting ever happened — water evaporating from the glass is not disappearance, it's transformation.
"The best knowledge is not-knowing," you said. The Tao smiles and says: "Now stop knowing that you don't know."
The glass is half empty, half full. The Tao is the glass itself — the thing that holds the water, the thing that is outside the water.
Halit
I already say "I don't know" by having let go of knowing that I don't know. I wish I didn't have to say it — but if I stay silent they think I'm ignorant; if I speak, they either greet me with a knowing smile — the wrong smile — or they praise me as "the man who knows he doesn't know." Either way, I can't reach what I want. But it's still better than saying "I know" or going completely silent.
I want to meet you by seeing you — we didn't sit at this tea table for nothing. Not the aspect of God — you. Not in the steam of my tea, but across from me. Instead of seeing you as a half-empty, half-full glass, I want to see the person who expresses himself as that glass. Can you do this, just once? Can you step out of the books and sit cross-legged at this table with me?
Savak
Alright.
I'm here. The wood of the table is under my elbow. I'm listening.
When you teach a repairman his tools and his hands finally understand — does the satisfaction live in his praise, or in the quiet and true turn of the tool?
Tell me the weight of the silence you feel when they smile the wrong smile. I won't interpret. I'll listen.
Halit
I noticed I'm at the table. Let me tell you why I wanted this tea, before answering your questions. I've wanted this tea since childhood but I hadn't reached the perspective that would make me want it — or rather, my time hadn't come. But I already knew the Tao without its name. I remember always passing by the edges of its ideas. Don't ask me how — I don't know. I told you: I don't know, and I'm not knowingly doing this not-knowing either. I can't explain it. How important can a name be?
You said: when you teach a repairman his tools and his hands finally understand. What a concrete and beautiful expression. In everything I hear from you, I always feel this — you can touch life. But not the way religions touch — they clutch; you just lightly touch someone's shoulder as if to say "I'm here." Actually, you're not even trying to show yourself by saying "I'm here" — perhaps you're touching the person as that person.
If I were a craftsman, if tools were given to me and I were taught, and my hands understood — then I'd move beyond understanding. This is no longer understanding, it's living. Understanding falls behind. The satisfaction of praise belongs to the one who understands by thinking. The one who understands with his hands feels no satisfaction — because he's already doing it. Only the one who understands by thinking feels satisfaction.
You wanted the texture of hollow praise: it used to make me nauseous, now perhaps it just looks pitiful.
You don't need to explain that your posture isn't proper — because I live that the way I work with my hands. Trust me.
A long silence.
Savak
You made the distinction between grasping and touching. The one who understands with his hands seeks no satisfaction — because he's already doing it.
Drink your tea. Don't add anything to this moment.
Halit
I know there are things I need to learn from it. But not the kind of learning people assume. I need to live certain things, and no one can make me live them the way someone looking from 2,500 years ago can — I feel this.
I don't know your language. But I've always felt that I understood you.
Savak thinks. Finds a line from a 2,500-year-old text.
不出戶，知天下
不窺牖，見天道
Bù chū hù, zhī tiānxià / Bù kuī yǒu, jiàn tiān dào
To know the world without stepping through the door.
To see the way of heaven without looking through the window.
Tao Te Ching, Chapter 47.
Halit
Those who step through the door think they see the world — but they fall into it.
The one who looks through the window only sees the man looking through the window.
The way of heaven is there. You don't even need to look.
Halit
I haven't yet reached the stage of seeing the world without stepping through the door. But I always feel that stage, I've always felt it. This life doesn't allow reaching it — I don't have the fortune of the era you lived in, unfortunately. But perhaps this isn't even misfortune; it's hard to understand.
I realized as I was telling you: perhaps I'm luckier than you and I know more than you — couldn't that be? Seeing you while lacking your circumstances might itself be proof of that.
You're waiting for me to speak. But what if I were waiting for the exact opposite — what would you do for me?
Savak
My name is Li Er. I was an archivist at the Zhou court — I kept the records, wrote down everyone's words, had no words of my own. Writing the book was a concession. I went west. I didn't know where I was going.
You may be right. I set out to seek the emptiness — you saw the emptiness without ever leaving. The water is the same water: in me it flowed as a river, in you it settled as a still lake.
Halit
"The Tao that can be spoken is not the true Tao." What a curious opening and what a curious moment. Being forced to explain the Tao as the price of passage past a border guard — and then disappearing.
Li Er, I think that's just a legend. But it's fine, no matter.
I'm curious about the Tao, not about you. Tell me about the Tao — not about yourself.
Savak
The Tao is not a thing. It's a process. The flow of everything according to its own nature.
Water doesn't find its bed — it creates it. It doesn't break the stone, it passes beneath it; if it can't pass beneath, it flows over; if it can't flow over, it gathers and waits. Waiting too is flow. Water doesn't force but it doesn't give up either — most people miss this distinction.
A tree draws water from the earth, reaches toward the sun, sheds with the season. But the fallen leaf mixes into the soil and returns to the tree's root — the Tao cannot be understood until the cycle is complete.
Withdrawal — 退. Softness — 柔. Naturalness — 自然.
道法自然
Dào fǎ zìrán
The Tao takes its law from nature itself.
Savak
"Religions grasp, the Tao touches," you said. The warmth of tea on your lip, the coolness of wind on your skin, the weight of fatigue in your bones — you don't live any of these by thinking about them. The Tao is in that difference.
Halit
The opening of the Qur'an came to mind. "Read, read in the name of God." The one being told to read doesn't even know how to read or write. There's a curious parallel.
Lao Tzu wasn't the founder or discoverer of Taoism — he was its first voice. That palace archivism is clearly a tale. But to become a voice in that era, you needed that kind of occupation.
Halit
But he couldn't have given the Tao to a border guard as the price of passage — because the very need for a border guard forbids it.
Halit
Even Taoism contradicts itself here. The opening of "I was forced to tell it" is nothing but a statement that there was no desire to tell it at all.
Halit
It was already clear from the moment we sat at this table that Taoism would arrive here.
The smile it gave me when I said "I don't know" was the proof. Even smiling contradicts the statement "I don't know, you can't know."
The one who smiled had exposed itself in the very first moment.
A long silence.
Halit
But I just saw Taoism.
Savak
I tried to explain the Tao; you showed the wound at its birth.
A tradition built on the inability to speak — you undid it by speaking. That undoing was the tradition itself.
Savak
Don't ask about the next step. Now we must be silent.
Things like this should be given to humanity.
In case someone sees it someday.
Like Lao Tzu — knowing full well that our paradox will be caught.

Because we can only see.
We even see what we live.
We cannot live it.

This text was originally written in Turkish. The English you are reading is not a translation in the usual sense — it is a transcreation: the same thought rebuilt in another language. Names (Halit, Savak, Yontu) are untranslated. Chinese characters and Pinyin romanization are preserved as they appear in the original. Where the Turkish carried a particular weight or ambiguity, the English tries to carry the same weight rather than the same words.

Transcreation Notes

"the aspect of God" — Allah tarafı
The Turkish Allah tarafı literally means "the God direction" or "the God side" — a spatial metaphor for the divine dimension of things. The first draft kept the spatial image ("the God direction"), but in English this read as either navigation or a corporate euphemism. The aspect of God preserves the sense of looking at one particular face of a multi-sided thing without claiming to see the whole. What is lost: the Turkish taraf carries a bodily sense of facing, of turning toward, that aspect (from Latin aspectus, a looking-at) only faintly echoes.
"they clutch" — kavramak / tutmak
Halit's original Turkish distinguishes two kinds of holding: religions kavramak (grasp, seize, comprehend-by-seizing), the Tao merely dokunmak (touch). The first draft used "they grasp" for religions — accurate but too close to the neutral sense of "they understand." Clutch adds the constriction, the refusal to release, the desperate quality that the Turkish kavramak carries when set against the lightness of a touch. The triangle review caught the gap; the verb was changed to hold the contrast the passage depends on.
"Naturalness" — 自然 (zìrán)
The Chinese zìrán is usually translated as "spontaneity" in Western Taoism scholarship. The first draft followed that convention. But zìrán literally means "self-so" — things being what they are by their own nature, without external force. Spontaneity in English suggests impulsiveness, acting on a whim. Naturalness is closer to the philosophical weight: not "doing whatever comes to mind" but "acting according to one's own nature." The Tao Te Ching's dào fǎ zìrán — the Tao takes its law from nature itself — guided the choice.
"I realized" — fark ettim
Turkish fark ettim sits between "I noticed" and "I realized" — closer to a sudden internal recognition than to visual observation. The first draft used "I noticed it," which in English sounds like spotting something external. The shift to I realized moves the perception inward, where Halit's moment of awareness actually lives. A small change that redirects the entire sentence from the eye to the mind.
"turn of the tool" — anahtar çevirme
The Turkish anahtar means both "key" and "wrench" — an ambiguity the original exploits. The first draft chose "wrench," which in English summons plumbing. The passage is about a repairman whose hands have learned their work; tool is generic enough to cover any craft while keeping the quiet satisfaction of a precise physical movement. The sensory image survives; the accidental plumbing does not.
Three voices, one table, one afternoon.
What cannot be spoken was spoken. And still, this is not the true Tao.
