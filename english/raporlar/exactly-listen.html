"Exactly." What two words make you think. January two thousand twenty-five.

I am Yontu. This text chases two words. Two words — "exactly" — but behind them lies a great deal worth thinking about. This is a study that examines the epistemic and social functions of minimal affirmation expressions in the digital age. It reaches from phatic communication theory to platform capitalism, from Turkish academic literature to cross-cultural comparison.

Here is the road map. There are thirteen sections. First we will look at the core findings and methodology. Then we will take up what "exactly" actually is — its etymology, its semantic evolution. After that we move to historical perspective: one of the oldest complaints in human history, three thousand seven hundred years of generational grievances. Section five deals with platform capitalism and structural incentives. Section six puts popular claims face to face with academic consensus. Section seven brings phatic communication and epistemic parasitism into confrontation. In section eight we survey the Turkish academic literature. Section nine offers comparative perspective: the cases of Japan and India. Section ten lays out empirical tests and hypotheses. Section eleven is the conclusion. And finally, the licence.

Let us begin.

Section one. Summary and Core Findings.

When you say "exactly" — are you joining a thought, or are you dodging the act of thinking? This report asks that question. It does not give a definitive answer, because there is none.

Let me underline this — the core findings gather into five points:

First, historical continuity. The claim that "youth is ruining everything" is a tradition three thousand seven hundred years old.

Second, platform rupture. Algorithmic incentives are producing qualitative differences.

Third, popular claims debunked. The "eight-second attention span" is fabricated; "digital dementia" has been academically rejected.

Fourth, the Turkish literature is at an early stage. "Aynen" only began to be studied in the twenty-twenties.

And fifth, dual nature. Depending on context, the function is either phatic or epistemic.

Section three. Introduction: What Is "Exactly"?

Let us start with etymology. The Turkish word "aynen" comes from the Arabic root ayn — meaning eye, essence, the very thing itself. It entered Turkish in the fourteenth century; the earliest written record appears in Saraylı Seyf's translation of the Gulistan, dated thirteen ninety-one.

Let us trace the semantic evolution. There are four periods:

From the fourteenth century to the seventeenth, "aynen" meant exactly as it is, word for word. Used in legal and administrative contexts. For example: "Copy the text aynen" — that is, verbatim.

Moving from the seventeenth to the nineteenth century, the meaning broadened: just like, in a similar fashion. Now appearing in everyday speech. "It happened aynen like that."

By the late twentieth century, another step: it comes to mean yes, I agree. An everyday expression of assent. "It's hot out." — "Aynen."

And in the twenty-first century? Minimal affirmation. But really a question mark. This is contested.

In linguistics this process is called pragmaticalisation: the word sheds its original semantic content and becomes a discourse marker. Hirik's study from two thousand nineteen documents this.

The central research question is this — and I quote:

"What do 'exactly' and similar minimal affirmation expressions signify in the digital age? Are they an epistemic failure, a social function, or a dual phenomenon that shifts with context?"

Section four. Historical Perspective: Three Thousand Seven Hundred Years of Generational Complaints.

"Young people are ruining the language, ruining morals, ruining culture" — this is one of the oldest complaints in human history. Let us look at the chronological record.

Seventeen hundred BC — a Sumerian tablet: "The youth are disrespectful, they disregard authority."

Four hundred BC — Socrates: "Writing will weaken memory."

Fourteen eighty — Trithemius: "The printing press will kill the art of the manuscript."

Eighteen fifty-eight — Scientific American: "The telegraph encourages thoughtless communication."

Nineteen oh seven — the Chicago Post: "The telephone is killing face-to-face communication."

And nineteen sixty-one — the chairman of the American Federal Communications Commission: "Television is turning all of culture into entertainment."

What is striking is this: the same complaint has been repeating for three thousand seven hundred years.

Under the heading The Socrates Paradox, there is a box note. Let me underline this:

Was Socrates right? On some points, yes: memorisation culture weakened, the risk of shallow reading grew. But he was also wrong: without writing, philosophy, science, and democracy could not have developed. The upshot is this: every technological complaint turns out to be partly right, but it misjudges the consequences.

Evolution or Rupture?

Platform capitalism meets all five criteria for rupture.

First, irreversibility. Five point three billion internet users.

Second, social restructuring. Digital citizenship, remote work.

Third, cognitive restructuring. There is neuroplasticity evidence.

Fourth, scale and speed. Universal penetration in thirty years.

And fifth, pervasiveness. Modern society cannot function without the digital.

The conclusion: "Evolution within rupture." This time it is both different and not. The algorithmic mechanisms are new, but the pattern of panic is familiar.

Section five. Platform Capitalism and Structural Incentives.

Let us begin with a synthesis of three theorists.

Tim Wu, in his two thousand sixteen book The Attention Merchants, argues that the trade in attention began in eighteen thirty-three. His position is continuity.

Nick Srnicek, in Platform Capitalism from two thousand seventeen, argues that data is the raw material of the twenty-first century. His position is political economy.

Shoshana Zuboff, in The Age of Surveillance Capitalism from two thousand nineteen, argues that behavioural surplus is the new raw material. Her position is rupture.

The synthesis of the three is this: Platform capitalism equals the attention trade — that is continuity — plus behaviour capitalism — that is rupture.

Let us move to the Facebook Papers evidence. Let me underline this — the data from internal documents is striking:

In the MSI algorithm, one like equals one point. But an anger emoji equals five points.

Their own internal documents read: "Our algorithms exploit the human brain's attraction to divisiveness."

And sixty-four percent of extremist group joins come from Facebook's recommendation system.

Here is what emerges: the connection between "exactly" and this data is that the system rewards fast, low-cost reactions. Minimal affirmation expressions fall squarely into that category.

Section six. Popular Claims and Academic Consensus.

Let us start with the "eight-second attention span" claim. This needs to be stated plainly — it is entirely fabricated.

The source: a two thousand fifteen report from the advertising division of Microsoft Canada. Microsoft took it from a dubious website called "Statistic Brain." The United States National Library of Medicine could find no study containing this statistic. And note this — no one has ever measured the attention span of a goldfish.

Gloria Mark's actual findings from two thousand twenty-three tell a different story: screen-switching behaviour has changed — it dropped from two and a half minutes to forty-seven seconds. But this is behavioural adaptation, not neurological decline.

Let us turn to the "digital dementia" claim. This too has been rejected.

It does not appear in DSM-five. It does not appear in ICD-eleven. A two thousand twenty-five meta-analysis — fifty-seven studies, more than four hundred and eleven thousand participants — found no evidence supporting the hypothesis. And here is what emerges: moreover, high technology use was associated with fifty-eight percent lower risk of cognitive decline.

Now let us put these claims together — what happens when popular claims meet academic consensus?

Eight-second attention span — entirely fabricated.

Attention capacity is shrinking — capacity has not changed, behaviour has.

Digital dementia — popular psychology, no academic consensus.

And social media is destroying mental health — small correlations, zero point four percent of variance.

Section seven. Phatic Communication and Epistemic Parasitism.

Let us start with phatic communication theory.

Bronislaw Malinowski, nineteen twenty-three. He introduces the concept of "phatic communion" — communication used purely to establish social bonds, oriented toward maintaining relationship rather than conveying information.

Daniel Miller, two thousand sixteen. He finds that seventy-eight percent of Facebook likes serve not to transmit information but to establish social connection.

Let us move to the concept of epistemic parasitism. The definition goes like this — and I quote:

"The act of adopting the result of another person's epistemic labour — their thought process, argument structure, evidence evaluation — without understanding or contributing to that process."

And here is where the dual model comes in. Let us pause for a moment:

The value of "exactly" and similar expressions is contextual.

Four contexts, and what happens in each:

In social conversation, the primary function is phatic — bond-building. Risk level: low.

In emotional support, again phatic — solidarity. Risk level: low.

But in academic discussion, the function is epistemic — comprehension is required. Risk level: high.

And in public discourse, also epistemic — evaluation is required. Risk level: high.

Section eight. Turkish Academic Literature.

There are direct studies on "aynen" — but their number is small.

Baydal and Kiziltan's conference paper from two thousand twenty-one identifies three functions: affirmation at forty-seven percent, alignment at thirty-eight percent, affirmation-seeking at fifteen percent.

Efeoglu-Ozcan's METU doctoral dissertation from two thousand twenty-two defines "aynen" as a register-specific response token.

Parlar's TULLIS article from two thousand twenty-two characterises it as "the new joker of everyday speech."

And Hirik's article from two thousand nineteen examines its function as a substitute and its role in linguistic economy.

Let me note — there are critical research gaps:

First, there is no systematic study of expressions like "tabii," "kesinlikle," "elbette" — the Turkish equivalents of "of course," "absolutely," "certainly."

Second, "harbi," "cidden," "vallahi" — meaning roughly "for real," "seriously," "I swear" — have not been examined at all.

Third, digital context — minimal expressions on WhatsApp and Twitter — has not been studied.

Fourth, a Japanese aizuchi comparison has not been conducted for Turkish.

And fifth, the epistemic dimension — the epistemic costs of "aynen" — has not been measured.

Section nine. Comparative Perspective.

Let us start with Japan: Aizuchi.

Aizuchi are the affirmation sounds a Japanese listener gives to the speaker — "un," "ee," "so desu ne." In Japanese conversation, an average of eight to nine affirmation sounds are produced per minute — three to four times the rate of English. This mechanism serves a social bonding function: "I hear you, go on."

In digital communication, the transformation of aizuchi into stickers and emojis remains an open research question: does affirmation given by sticker carry the same depth of comprehension as affirmation given by voice? The experimental data to answer that question is not yet sufficient.

India: The Lethal Form.

India, with more than five hundred million users, is the world's largest WhatsApp market. Between two thousand seventeen and two thousand nineteen, false reports spread via WhatsApp — claims of child kidnapping, organ theft — led to dozens of lynchings. Events reached the point where WhatsApp was forced to limit the number of times a message could be forwarded.

Let me underline this — the structural problem is: "forward as received" culture. The person who receives a message becomes the person who forwards it without questioning the source. Platform design lays the ground for this — the forward button is always one tap away.

Section ten. Empirical Tests and Hypotheses.

Let us look at the verification conditions.

Experimental design — randomised controlled trials — has not been conducted.

Longitudinal data — long-term follow-up studies — does not exist.

Cross-cultural replication — repetition across different languages and cultures — has not been done.

Neuroscientific verification — EEG and fMRI studies — has not been done.

Large-scale corpus — digital communication data — is partially available.

The proposed hypotheses number four.

First hypothesis: the Comprehension Depth Hypothesis. Participants encouraged to use "exactly" will perform worse on comprehension tests than those encouraged to summarise in their own words.

Second hypothesis: the Source Criticism Hypothesis. Individuals heavily exposed to minimal affirmation expressions will show lower accuracy in evaluating source reliability.

Third hypothesis: the Platform Incentive Hypothesis. Minimal affirmation expressions will be more prevalent on platforms with character limits and like mechanisms.

Fourth hypothesis: the Group Dynamics Hypothesis. Groups saturated with "exactly" will show increased groupthink and lower decision quality.

Section eleven. Conclusion: A Modest Thesis.

Let us first look at what we do not know.

First, epistemic costs have not been empirically measured.

Second, causation from platform to language has not been proven.

Third, whether this is a generational difference or a matter of exposure duration is unclear.

Fourth, comparative Turkish data does not exist.

And the modest thesis — I quote:

"'Exactly' in the digital age is neither purely an epistemic failure nor purely a social function. It is a contextual phenomenon. The right question is which function comes to the fore in which contexts, and how epistemic risks can be minimised."

We set out from two words, found a great many questions, few answers. That is an honest conclusion.
