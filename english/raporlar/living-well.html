Living Well

Çetin Altan

Milliyet, 24 September 2014 · Şeytanın Gör Dediği

Everyone wants to live well. I used to think so too. Then I began to sense that the meaning of living well changes from person to person.

For me, for instance, living well has been making the most of time — wringing from it the most unforgettable taste that circumstance will permit.

Living well is not merely a matter of money. It is grabbing a raging bull by the horns on a whim and forcing it to its knees.

How this is done varies from person to person, from life to life.

For some, living well is ordering a strong tea in the ward of the Sağmacılar.

For some, living well is chasing love down roads no one has taken, going all the way to Afghanistan, and flying back to Istanbul alone with nothing but a shattered crystal glass.

For some, living well is talking about London in the parks of Tokyo.

For some, living well is drinking champagne on a night when every last hope has gone to hell.

For some, living well is neither fitting inside the ring of marriage nor complaining about being alone.

Everyone wants to live well.

So you think.

Generally, people want to be conspicuous within a secure life.

For some, living well is recognising the freedom to go to hell without getting angry at anyone, without disrespecting anyone — and granting that freedom to the kind of conscience that can appreciate it.

For some, living well is getting bored of international summits and taking a gondola ride in Venice.

For some, living well is reducing everything to zero and starting life over ten times.

For some, living well is simply walking out on a whim.

For some, living well is thinking of finding an orchestra for a French violinist who plays sonatas beautifully, so that she might play concertos and symphonies too.

For some, living well is being polite while swearing, and swearing while being polite.

For some, living well is leaving everything deemed "important" to those who believe in its importance, and sailing paper boats in the details no one cares about.

For some, living well is reciting poetry at every turn, warranted or not.

For some, living well is truly loving.

For some, living well is being strong enough to stand against those who do not know how to live well.

They say everyone loves living well.

If they loved it that much, they would live.

People have not yet even learned what it means to have lived well…

They wear out their days endlessly pulling the three or four beads on their little prayer strings — terrified that the thread might snap.

Musset said his only faithful friend was solitude.

So did Can Yücel.

I understand them both.

Perhaps living well is understanding.

Especially when what is understood is a solitude as accomplished as a stone dropped into a well…

Why I carry this text

This text found me at a time when life had been reset to zero — not during a deliberate search, not by accident either, but through a newspaper clipping someone pressed into my hand, in one of those intervals where neither what was left behind nor what lay ahead had a name yet. Sometimes a person does not stumble upon a text; the text finds the person. That is the inexplicable side of what we call timing.

I know a man who has started life at least ten times. Each time from scratch, each time learning something different, but each time losing the same thing: the previous life itself. Altan writes, "for some, living well is reducing everything to zero and starting life over ten times," and everyone who reads this sentence projects their own film in their head — but if you know that man's life, the sentence stops being a metaphor and becomes a record. Every fresh start looks like a story of courage from outside; from the inside, every reset is a fire site — a place where everything left behind has turned to ash.

The reason I wanted to carry Altan's text is that he left "living well" undefined. This is a text that shows rather than defines — it circles a concept, touches it, releases it, touches it again, each time from a different angle, but nowhere does it say "living well is this." This is a methodological choice: showing carries a more honest epistemology than defining, because the "for some" formula is itself a confession — there is no single truth, there are as many ways of living as there are points of view. Altan was one of those rare writers who knew this and carried the knowledge with composure.

The last two lines work differently every time they are read. "Perhaps living well is understanding" — the first time I read it, it was a conclusion, the point where the text gathered itself. In later readings it became something else: the verb to understand appeared as an act, not a passive grasp but an active effort; the person who understands looks calm on the surface, but underneath there is a machine running constantly, one that ceaselessly translates the outer world and the inner world, a machine that lets through as much as it filters. "Especially when what is understood is a solitude as accomplished as a stone dropped into a well" — this sentence sounds like poetry the first time; the second time, you hear the single sound a stone makes falling into a well — unrequited, irreversible, its echo. Accomplished solitude: a solitude that sustains itself, expects no approval from outside, sits quietly at the bottom of the well.

This text belongs to Çetin Altan. The reason it is carried here is not possession but a belief that a text should stand in the right place — some writings should be placed somewhere so they do not remain on newspaper pages, because newspapers decay, but thoughts live wherever they are carried.
