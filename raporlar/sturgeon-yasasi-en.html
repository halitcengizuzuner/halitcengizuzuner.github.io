1. Source: Theodore Sturgeon

Theodore Sturgeon (1918-1985), American science fiction writer. Over 400 reviews, more than 120 short stories, 11 novels. Won the International Fantasy Award for More Than Human (1953). Wrote scripts for the original Star Trek series.

"He was the most influential science fiction writer from the 1940s to the 1960s." — Samuel R. Delany

Sturgeon stood apart among the science fiction writers of his era: he was virtually the only one tackling themes of loneliness, love, sexuality, hermaphroditism, and homosexuality. His protagonists were typically repressed young people.

2. How the Law Came About

2.1 Context

In the 1950s, science fiction was dismissed as "Buck Rogers stuff." Critics would cherry-pick the worst examples and declare that "90% of science fiction is crud," rejecting the entire genre wholesale.

2.2 The Original Statement (September 1953, Philadelphia WorldCon)

"When people talk about the mystery novel, they mention The Maltese Falcon and The Big Sleep. When they talk about the western, they mention The Way West and Shane. But when they talk about science fiction, they call it 'that Buck Rogers stuff,' and they say 'ninety percent of science fiction is crud.' Well, they're right. Ninety percent of science fiction is crud. But then ninety percent of everything is crud, and it's the ten percent that isn't crud that is important. And the ten percent of science fiction that isn't crud is as good as or better than anything being written anywhere." — As recounted by James Gunn

2.3 Written Form (March 1958, Venture Science Fiction)

"I repeat Sturgeon's Revelation, which was wrung out of me after twenty years of wearying defense of science fiction against attacks of people who used the worst examples of the field for ammunition."

"Revelation: Ninety percent of everything is crud." — Theodore Sturgeon, Venture Science Fiction, March 1958

2.4 Two Distinct Formulations

ℹ️ Two Formulations
  • Sturgeon's Revelation: "Ninety percent of everything is crud."
  • Sturgeon's Law: "Nothing is always absolutely so."

The second is the meta-principle of the first: even the 90% figure is not absolute.

3. The Structure of the Argument

Sturgeon's defense follows a four-step chain of reasoning:

1

Concession

90% of science fiction is crud — direct admission, no denial.

2

Generalization

90% of everything is crud — science fiction is not special in this regard.

3

Conclusion

Judging a genre by its worst examples is a logical error.

4

Claim

A genre should be judged by its best work.

✓ Type of Argument

This is a defensive argument: it does not glorify what it defends — it dismantles the basis of the attack.

5. Echoes — Related Concepts

5.1 The Pareto Principle (80/20)

Vilfredo Pareto, 1896: 80% of Italy's land was owned by 20% of its population. Joseph Juran adapted this to quality control: "80% of problems stem from 20% of causes."

ℹ️ Sturgeon and Pareto

Sturgeon and Pareto observe the same phenomenon from different angles: Pareto measures where value concentrates, Sturgeon measures the crud ratio. Both demonstrate that normal distribution does not apply.

5.2 Power Law Distributions

Quality does not follow a normal distribution; it follows a power law. A small number of outputs generate disproportionate impact. In this kind of distribution, the median sits far below the mean — most output falls "below average."

5.3 Gresham's Law: Bad Drives Out Good

Monetary origin: Sir Thomas Gresham, 16th century. When debased currency enters circulation, sound currency is hoarded and disappears from the market.

Modern applications: Clickbait drives out quality journalism. Fast fashion kills sustainable producers. Algorithmic content renders original work invisible.

⚠ A Critical Distinction

Sturgeon says the quality distribution is natural. Gresham argues that quality degradation is driven by institutional dynamics — bad actively displaces good.

Sturgeon describes; Gresham explains the mechanism.

5.4 The Dunning-Kruger Effect

Kruger & Dunning (1999): Participants performing in the bottom quartile estimated their own performance at the 62nd percentile. Their actual performance: 12th percentile.

⚠ The Sturgeon Connection

Those who produce the 90% are often unaware that what they produce is crud. Dunning-Kruger explains why Sturgeon's 90% is so stubbornly persistent: producers mistake their own output for something of value.

5.5 Taleb: Signal and Noise

Nassim Nicholas Taleb's analysis in Fooled by Randomness and The Black Swan:

Observation Frequency Signal Noise
Yearly 50% 50%
Daily 5% 95%
Hourly 0.5% 99.5%

As observation frequency increases, the noise ratio explodes. Sturgeon's 90% is equivalent to Taleb's "noise": the majority is not signal, it is noise.

6. Counterarguments and Limitations

6.1 The 90% Figure Is Arbitrary

True, it is rhetorical — a stand-in for "the vast majority." But the order of magnitude holds: most data points fall in the 60-97% range, confirming that the observation "most of everything is mediocre" is valid.

6.2 Survivorship Bias

Looking back from the present, earlier eras appear more "quality-rich." Three reasons for this:

  • The filter of time: The crud that time has sifted out becomes invisible; the surviving 10% becomes "the era."
  • The nostalgia filter: Personally curated samples do not represent the whole.
  • The import filter: Only the best of other cultures reaches us.

6.3 Domain Dependence

In fields with high barriers to entry, the ratio drops (medicine: 10-15% error). In fields with low barriers, it climbs (apps: 62% unused). Wherever digitization lowers the barrier to entry, the crud ratio rises accordingly.

6.4 Who Defines "Crud"?

Quality is subjective. There are multiple axes of quality: commercial success, critical acclaim, lasting impact, technical competence. What counts as "crud" on one axis may be valuable on another.

6.5 Does the Ratio Change Over Time?

"90% may be too low an estimate." — Tom Nichols, The Death of Expertise

Digitization lowered the barrier to production, and the volume of crud exploded. Even if the ratio stays the same, the absolute number has multiplied.

6.6 Is the 90% Necessary?

The innovation literature suggests that failure generates knowledge. Success does not come without experimentation, and most experiments fail.

💡 Key Insight

Eliminate the 90% and the 10% vanishes too — because the 10% emerges from the 90%.

An evolutionary analogy: 99% of mutations are harmful or neutral, but remove the pool and adaptation halts. The 90% is not waste — it is the trial ground.

7. The Deeper Insight — The Crud Ratio Does Not Diminish the Field

7.1 Sturgeon's Own Argument

This was the law's purpose all along: the fact that 90% of science fiction is crud does not make the genre a failure. The same is true of every field. Judging a genre by its worst examples is a simple logical error.

7.2 The Ecological Argument

The vast majority of mutations are harmful or neutral. But the pool is essential: beneficial mutations can only arise from a large field of trials. Failure is the precondition of success.

7.3 Selection Mechanisms

Four mechanisms extract value from the crud pool:

  • Editorial filters: Peer review, curatorial selection, editorial standards.
  • Market filters: Consumer choice, competition, price signals.
  • The filter of time: Lasting impact, endurance across generations.
  • Community filters: Expert consensus, canon formation, collective memory.

7.4 The Real Danger

When Gresham is combined with Taleb's noise analysis, the real risk comes into focus:

⚠ The Algorithmic Gresham Effect

When algorithms reward the 90%, the 10% still exists — but it becomes unfindable.

The problem is not the crud ratio itself — that is natural. The problem is the corruption of selection mechanisms: clickbait algorithms measure engagement, not quality, and engagement is generally inversely proportional to quality. When the filter breaks, Sturgeon's natural 90% transforms into Gresham's active displacement mechanism.

8. Conclusion

"Sturgeon's Law is neither consolation nor cynicism. It is an arithmetic of observation."

Three Core Takeaways

1. The majority is mediocre everywhere. This is not an alarm — it is background noise. The alarm should sound when mediocrity begins to drive out quality (Gresham). In academic publishing, 82% of papers go uncited; in music, 86% of tracks go unheard — these are not pathological, they are natural. The alarm: when that 86% is algorithmically promoted ahead of the 14%.

2. The fact that 90% is crud does not diminish the power of the 10%. Most cases in a legal system are unremarkable, but the force of precedent-setting decisions is unaffected by this. The diagnostic error rate in medicine is 10-15%, but the life-saving power of a correct diagnosis is not reduced by it. 64% of psychology studies fail to replicate, but the contribution of the 36% that do replicate to human knowledge is not thereby lessened.

3. But the existence of the 90% makes finding the 10% harder. This practical problem is the least discussed yet most important dimension of the law.

"Don't waste your time booing the crud. Go after the good stuff, or walk away." — Daniel Dennett (loosely paraphrased)
