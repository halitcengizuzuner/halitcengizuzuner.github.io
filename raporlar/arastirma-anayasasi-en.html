§ Preamble

Where we stand, and why

"The existence of a general rule of method would block progress."
— Paul Feyerabend

Why

Research done by people answering to their own curiosity. No ambition to contribute to science, no titles, no institutional stakes. Ask the question. Find the honest answer.

How

Not rules — principles. Each inquiry finds its own path. No universal method recipe. The guiding principles were distilled from inside mistakes.

Limit

The best result reachable, not the best result possible. "There is always someone better than you." The stopping point is found by intuition.

Measure

Measured by what it adds to the person. Not by academic standards. But the academy's demand for originality and critical distance serves as inspiration.

Fig. 1 — Researcher distribution. Structure sets the floor of depth.

The researcher's quality determines the research's standard. Everyone satisfies curiosity differently. Some accept surface-level answers; others cannot rest until they have seen the thing from inside. This distinction is not trivial: the researcher's structure sets the lower bound of depth — not tools, time, or resources.

Two people answering the same question can produce results that differ in quality by an abyss. Without knowing the researcher's quality, you cannot know the research's quality.

Every principle in this document comes from somewhere: from mistakes. Work produced over the years that, when revisited, prompted the question "why did this fail?" — each taught something. Behind every principle written here, a silent mistake stands watching.

§ Framework Principle

Living Method

Every inquiry finds its own path.

No universal method recipe. Principles are fixed; their application varies each time. Research rests on principles, not recipes.

Research has a property — actually everything in life has it. If a person can enter it and live it, the research begins speaking to that person. Enter it and you begin to live it; begin to live it and the research looks back at you.

This work moves like an orchestra: the human wonders, steers by intuition, corrects; the AI scans, assembles, questions. Not a conductor-musician hierarchy — mutual guidance. The principles themselves were born through this orchestra.

"Practice before formulation: the research method began to be lived before it had a name."
§ Principles

14 Principles, 5 Clusters

I. Source Use

01 Primary Source Priority

Whatever the subject, start with the object's own texts. Researching legal language? Read the legal texts first, then those who write about them. The reverse always produces distorted results.

Lived directly: searching "critique of legal language" returned linguists, sociologists, bloggers — not the legal texts themselves. Six thousand words built on outside observers went to waste. Starting from the primary legislation changed everything.

But looking at primary sources is not enough. You need to look from inside their perspective. This is not passive reading — it is entering the logic of that world.

Researching legal language through a linguist's eyes yields "archaic," "complex." Through a lawyer's eyes: "precise," "comprehensive." Neither is wrong; neither is complete. Entering the subject means understanding why the language works the way it does — from within its own logic.

02 Source Position Mapping

Every source has a perspective. Using a source without identifying that perspective inherits its blindness. "Where does this person stand, and from where are they looking?" must be the first question.

Linguists found legal language "complex" — looking from a language perspective. Lawyers found it "precise" — looking from a legal perspective. Neither was wrong; neither was complete. A prolific textbook author is not automatically an authority on legal language. Title does not confer expertise.

Distilled from: Legal language research + language sessions
03 Verification Reflex

Claims are verified regardless of who makes them. Source credibility attaches to the claim, not the person. This is not distrust — it is method. The biggest scandals in academic history emerged exactly where the verification reflex was absent.

04 Concrete Citation

No abstract citation. If you cite a decision, a text, a datum — you have reached the thing itself. The citation must have a real text behind it.

In AI-produced research, abstract citations are the primary source of hallucination. The model wrote a case number — is it real? Is it relevant? Without the text: not a claim, a rumor.

Distilled from: Legal research infrastructure — direct access to primary court databases

II. Production

05 Originality

Every piece of research must contain something new: new data, new context, new synthesis, new interpretation, correction of existing knowledge — at least one. Being accidentally right is not enough; the contribution must be traceable.

Sturgeon's Law report: The concept was Sturgeon's, but the Pareto + Gresham + Dunning-Kruger connection was original.
Phatic communication report: The phatic communication + epistemic parasitism synthesis was original.

06 Opening the Problem

Not defending a thesis — opening a problem. A thesis can emerge from inside the problem, but the problem must be larger than the thesis. The question "under what conditions could this be wrong?" must be asked.

Richard Feynman described this in his 1974 "Cargo Cult Science" lecture: Pacific islanders wore wooden headphones, posted guards at the runway, mimicked aircraft — performing every ritual. The planes just didn't land. Research can look the same way: forms, procedures, tables — but the most important thing absent. If "under what conditions is this wrong?" is never asked, you are doing Cargo Cult Science.

Distilled from: Assessment of beta reports + Feynman "Cargo Cult Science" (1974)

III. Process

07 Speed–Depth Balance

Accumulation is not thinking. The gathering phase and the analysis phase must be separated. Scanning thirty sources in ten minutes is impressive — but being impressive is not depth.

Diederik Stapel, Professor at Tilburg University, published fifty-eight papers through 2011 without conducting any field research. The numbers he gave students as "raw data" were entirely fabricated. Exposure was accidental: a student joked "these data fit the hypothesis too perfectly." The joke was the truth. Fifty-eight papers retracted. Speed produces volume; volume creates the illusion of depth.

Negative: Stapel case (Tilburg, 2011) — Positive: Darwin (24 years, 1 book)
08 Iterative Progress

Don't delete the first draft — build on top of it. Even if wrong, record the mistake, understand why it failed, let the next step stand on the mistake. Ninety percent being garbage is necessary for ten percent to exist.

This document: v0.1, v0.2, v0.3, v0.4, v0.5 — each version built on the last. The previous ones sit in the git repository as retrievable references.

09 Map-First Work

Research is not collecting information — it is drawing a map. Information is the material of the map, not the map itself. Relationships between materials, their contradictions, their gaps — the map makes these visible.

Distilled from: "You used to spend days drawing maps before you wrote a brief"
Principle 01 · Deep Dive

Entering the Subject

Looking at primary sources is not enough. You need to look from inside where that source looks. Not passive observation — entering the logic of that world. Throughout history, the people who applied this most concretely were not researchers — they were actors, journalists, and scientists.

1960s · USA
Frank Abagnale Jr. — Four Identities
Pan Am Pilot · Doctor · Lawyer · Bank Teller

In the 1960s Frank Abagnale worked as a Pan Am pilot, a Louisiana doctor, a Georgia attorney, and a bank teller — without any training. During the years before he was caught, he did not merely use false identities; he entered each one.

To become a pilot, he called Pan Am's uniform supplier by phone. He bought a Pan Am model from a toy store, removed the badge, attached it to a fake ID. He boarded a plane and watched how pilots greeted each other — and copied it. He learned the word "deadheading" by overhearing a conversation on his first flight.

When he became a doctor, he noted down terms he didn't know, researched them overnight, used them the next morning. "What do you think of this procedure?" — and listened carefully, copying body language. When he became a lawyer, he genuinely studied for the bar exam — and passed on the third attempt.

Abagnale's striking quality: what made the documents work was not the documents themselves. It was his internalizing how each world looked from inside — how pilots walk, how physicians look at patients, how attorneys speak to colleagues.

Note: A significant portion of Abagnale's story is historically contested. But the film and book present a compelling narrative for illustrating how "entering the subject" works.

1974 · Arizona
Temple Grandin — Seeing Through the Cow's Eyes
Engineer · Ethologist · Autism

In the early 1970s, a university student was doing something that would change the livestock industry: crawling. Literally crawling on the ground through slaughterhouse ramps, moving through the corridors cows walked on, on her knees.

Temple Grandin was an autistic researcher. Why do cattle go berserk on ramps? Engineers categorized it as "animal behavior" and recommended stronger ramps. Grandin looked from inside the ramps — literally.

And she saw: floor reflections were signaling threat. Lighting angles were triggering flight responses. The turn angle made animals feel forward movement was impossible. None of the engineers had seen any of this — because none had crawled on the ground.

Grandin's solution: serpentine corridors, shadow-eliminating lighting, floor texture changes. Today more than fifty percent of US and Canadian meat processing facilities operate with equipment Grandin designed.

Autism was the tool: Grandin says she thinks in visual and sensory data, like animals. Not analysis from outside — look from within.

1929 · Paris
George Orwell — The Kitchen Beneath Paris
Writer · Journalist · Hôtel X Dishwasher

1929, Paris. George Orwell began working not as a journalist, but as a plongeur — dishwasher. In the kitchen basement of Hôtel X, six days a week, twelve hours a day. Copper pots, steam, accumulated grease.

The great hotels of Paris look glamorous from the surface. From inside — from the basement — a different world. Orwell saw: the meals reaching wealthy guests' tables came from a basement with rotting wooden floors, insects on the walls, damp throughout.

Orwell was there not as an observer but as a worker. This distinction is critical: an observer can leave whenever they want; a worker cannot. The inability to leave makes remaining necessary — and necessity makes seeing possible.

What he learned was not abstract poverty analysis — it was the mental state of the plongeur: "the closing of the capacity for political thought through exhaustion." This insight is only visible to someone who came from inside.

"Down and Out in Paris and London" (1933) was the product of this experience.

1931 · England
George Orwell — Walking as a Tramp
Yorkshire · Southern England · 49 Tramps

1931. Orwell deliberately wandered England as a tramp in old clothes. "The Spike" (1931) was born from this experience.

Waiting for a spike (workhouse) with a column of forty-nine. Upon entering: clothing taken, raw cotton shirt given, a greasy towel shared between six for three-minute baths. Meal: bread and tea.

The most striking observation: waste containers full of meat and bread from hotel kitchens, two hundred meters from a half-starved tramp crowd. "This was deliberate policy."

He could have said from outside "poverty is dire." What he grasped from inside was different: exhaustion deprives people of the capacity to produce ideology. This insight became praxis in "1984."

1983–1985 · West Germany
Günter Wallraff — Two Years as Ali
German Journalist → Turkish Migrant Worker

1983. German journalist Günter Wallraff took on the identity of a Turkish migrant. He named himself Ali Sigirlioğlu, dyed his hair, wore dark contact lenses, practiced an accent. His Turkish would be broken — Greek mother, Turkish father, raised in Piraeus.

For two years he took the hardest jobs in West Germany: McDonald's kitchen, Thyssen steel plant, nuclear waste transport, aluminum smelting. He worked as Ali everywhere.

What he saw: no protective equipment. Accidents not reported. Overtime unpaid. Managers publicly humiliating Ali for knowing something other than German. Workers banned from speaking outside toilet breaks — for speaking Turkish.

The book's final scene: a temp agency boss asked Wallraff to select five Turkish workers to be exposed to dangerous radiation in a malfunctioning nuclear plant. Wallraff revealed his identity.

"Ganz Unten" (1985): 4 million copies, 30 languages. An outside researcher would have been stopped at the factory gate. Inside, Ali saw everything.

Stanislavski · Day-Lewis · De Niro
Method Actors — Living It, Not Playing It
Theatre · Cinema

Stanislavski's system said this: an actor does not represent a role — they live it. "Magic If" — "What would I feel if I were in this character's circumstances?" Not outside analysis — lived experience from within.

Daniel Day-Lewis refused to leave his wheelchair throughout filming My Left Foot (1989). Set crew fed him. Result: two broken ribs from the forward curvature of his spine — and an Oscar.

For There Will Be Blood (2007): months of isolation in a California desert cabin reading oil drilling sources. His voice — that John Huston-esque voice soaked in oil and greed — found through obsessive months of experiment.

Robert De Niro became a member of the New York Taxi Drivers' Union for Taxi Driver (1976). Obtained a real taxi license. Before filming, worked twelve-hour weekend shifts — no camera, no set. Only one passenger recognized him; the passenger was another actor.

The "method acting" approach in research: even where full immersion is impossible, prioritizing sources that look from inside that world — learning its language, rituals, logic — comes from the same principle.

1960 · Gombe, Tanzania
Jane Goodall — Earning the Chimpanzee's Trust
Primatologist · 1 Year of Patient Presence

July 14, 1960. Jane Goodall arrived at Gombe Stream in Tanzania, twenty-six years old, no formal zoology degree. Louis Leakey had chosen her deliberately as someone free of preconceptions.

At first the chimpanzees fled. About a year later, a male she named David Greybeard began to accept her presence.

November 4, 1960: Goodall observed David Greybeard at a termite mound. The animal was inserting a grass stem into a termite hole and withdrawing it — with termites clinging on. A specially modified object — tool use. Until that day, attributed exclusively to humans.

Goodall telegraphed Louis Leakey. His response is famous: "Now we must redefine tool, redefine man, or accept chimpanzees as humans."

This discovery was possible because Goodall worked with patient, non-patronizing, existential closeness — no rushing, no sudden movements. Not scientific distance: trust from within.

"Entering the subject is not just observing — it is internalizing that world's language, rituals, shortcuts, and how it looks from inside."
Principle 03 · Deep Dive

Verification Reflex — History's Most Expensive Mistakes

The cost of the absent verification reflex is not abstract — it is measurable.

Academic Fraud

Andrew Wakefield — The MMR Vaccine and Autism Lie

A 1998 paper in The Lancet, based on twelve childhood cases, claimed a causal link between MMR vaccine and autism. Data were fabricated. The paper remained published twelve years.

Result: England's vaccination rate fell from 92% to 73%, in some parts of London to 50%. Measles outbreaks erupted. Deaths followed.

In 2010 the research was found entirely invented. Wakefield lost his license. "Published" had been sufficient — verification never came.

The Lancet, 1998 / retraction: 2010
Journalism Scandal

Jayson Blair — The New York Times's Internal Collapse

A young New York Times reporter wrote thirty-six articles — without researching any. He never spoke to sources, never visited locations, fabricated verbatim quotes.

For years nobody verified. The newspaper's own investigation exposed it. This shows how institutional credibility erodes the verification reflex.

NYT, 2003
Refusal to Verify

Semmelweis — Medicine Refused to Wash Its Hands

1847. When Ignaz Semmelweis made chlorine solution handwashing mandatory, mortality fell in one month from 18% to 2.2%. The data were there.

The medical establishment refused — because acceptance would mean admitting doctors were killing patients with their own hands. "Semmelweis reflex" is today a cognitive science concept: automatic rejection of new information because it conflicts with existing beliefs.

Vienna Hospital, 1847
Small Scale

Everyday Verification Examples

"Catch Me If You Can" lead: Said to be "Brad Pitt" — verified: Leonardo DiCaprio.

Film year: Assumed to be one year — verified: different year entirely.

Darwinist "natural selection" in popular use: Often misattributed or misquoted — the actual sentence is different.

Small mistakes originate from the same principle as great ones: no verification reflex.

Occurred in these sessions

John Ioannidis published "Why Most Published Research Findings Are False" in PLOS Medicine in 2005. Methodological errors, small sample sizes, publication bias — findings could not be replicated. Without the verification reflex, "published" was sufficient. No longer.

Principle 10 · Language Fidelity · Deep Dive

Night on Earth

JIM JARMUSCH · 1991 · Five Cities · Same Night · Five Language Problems

Los Angeles

19:07 PST · 7 PM

Corky — Winona Ryder Victoria — Gena Rowlands

Corky is seventeen, driving a cab into the night. Victoria is a Hollywood casting director, going from the airport into the city. During the ride Victoria sees something in Corky — an energy, a raw talent — and offers her an acting career. Corky listens, smiles, and declines. She wants to be a mechanic.

This segment is not a class conflict — something more subtle. What Victoria offers is not a world Corky cannot imagine; she can imagine it, but doesn't want it. Her way of saying this comes in street speech.

The original saying: "Can't live with 'em, can't live without 'em."
Corky's version: "Can't live with 'em, can't shoot 'em."

She changes the second half — doesn't fully know the original but keeps going as if she does. The broken idiom is the character. Preserving the fracture is part of meaning transfer. Smooth it over and you erase Corky herself.

New York

22:07 EST · Approaching Midnight

Helmut — Armin Mueller-Stahl Yo-Yo — Giancarlo Esposito Angela — Rosie Perez

Helmut is East German, a former circus clown. His first working day in New York. He tries to pick up Yo-Yo but cannot operate automatic transmission — East Germany didn't have automatic cars. Yo-Yo takes the wheel and drives his own driver.

Yo-Yo: "New York is cool."
Helmut: "Yes, it is cold."
Yo-Yo: "No — cool! Hip! Happening!"

The cultural meaning of "cool" is only accessible from inside that language. It isn't in the dictionary — it's in context.

Even names can't be translated: Yo-Yo laughs when he hears "Helmut." Helmut can't make sense of "Yo-Yo." Each finds the other's "normal" name absurd. Beneath the language barrier something else: a worldview barrier.

A connection is still forged — but it comes not from perfect word transfer, but from both being marginal. Yo-Yo doesn't get cabs because he's Black; Helmut is foreign to the system because he's German.

Paris

04:07 CET · Before Dawn

Driver — Isaach de Bankolé (Ivory Coast) Blind Woman — Béatrice Dalle

An Ivorian driver picks up two drunk diplomats. When they hear the driver is from Ivory Coast, the diplomats laugh:

"Il y voit rien" — "He can't see a thing."

In French, "Ivoirien" (Ivorian) and "il y voit rien" (he can't see a thing) share a sound similarity — a linguistic weapon layered with post-colonial contempt.

This wordplay compresses the intra-African hierarchy of post-colonial France into a single sentence. The driver throws the diplomats out.

Then he picks up a blind woman. The dialogue between them revolves on this axis: the driver frames blindness as a loss. The woman rejects this — saying that seeing can be a burden, that perception is not the eye's monopoly.

The meaning embedded in language: "il voit rien" means both "he doesn't see" and "he's blind" — the phrase used against the driver also defines the woman's situation. Translating requires choosing the double meaning — and the choice changes the scene's meaning entirely.

Rome

04:07 CET · Same Moment, Different City

Gino — Roberto Benigni Priest — Paolo Bonacelli

Gino picks up a priest late at night. The priest is ill, barely walking. Gino wants to confess his sins. The priest says "I am not on official duty." Gino doesn't hear it — and begins.

Starting with minor sins.
A cascading sequence of increasingly graphic, increasingly absurd confessions.
The priest grows paler, reaches for his nitroglycerin.
The pill falls on a bump in the road.
The priest dies.

Benigni's Florentine accent and street Italian carry layers of meaning to Italian audiences. In translation, this layer falls away.

Language becomes an involuntary weapon. Gino didn't intend to kill a priest — but what he said was lethal. Two speech systems collided: southern Italy's sunny tradition of confession and northern European moral language. Benigni spoke not because he was confessing but because his voice came out. The passing of meaning was never part of the calculation.

Helsinki

05:07 EET · –10°C · Snow

Mika — Matti Pellonpää Drunks — Kaurismäki ensemble

The final segment: the shortest and the heaviest. Three drunk men, a tired driver. Jarmusch gave the characters the names Aki and Mika — a tribute to Finnish cinema's two great figures, Aki and Mika Kaurismäki.

The drunks tell Mika about their friend: yesterday he lost his job, his new car is totaled, his wife wants a divorce, it came out his daughter is pregnant. Four disasters.

Mika: "Is that all?"

The most concise expression of Nordic suffering.

No lament, no performance of empathy. Direct competition: my story is heavier.

Mika tells his: They had no children for years. One day his wife became pregnant. The baby was premature, put in an incubator. Doctors said it would live a week. Mika didn't let himself attach — if it were lost, they'd both be destroyed. The baby survived. His wife said: "Our child needs your love too." Mika attached. The baby died.

Final scene: dawn breaking. Mika slowly drives around an empty park. Alone. Nothing said. Tom Waits's music is sparse, the bell tones few — it frames the silence, does not fill it.

In Finnish, the weight of what is unspoken is greater than what is spoken. Carrying this into another language is possible — but noting the frame is essential.

Language does not guarantee meaning. Speaking the same language (Corky and Victoria) does not produce understanding; speaking different languages (Helmut and Yo-Yo) does not prevent it. Communication fails most completely in Rome (it doesn't exist), and in Helsinki takes place almost without words.

Language fidelity crystallizes here: transferring the source language is not enough. Transferring the worldview embedded in that language — including its fractures, misunderstandings, deliberate breaks — is required. Otherwise it's not translation; it's summary.

Principles 11–14 · Attitude

Antiperformance & Skin in the Game

Antiperformance is rare in academic culture. Darwin did it.

Galápagos to publication

0 Mendel's
pea plants
0 Copies Mendel
distributed

Darwin visited the Galápagos in 1835. Five weeks. He published On the Origin of Species in 1859. Twenty-four years. Not a single paper in between, no attempt to persuade anyone. He moved when Wallace arrived at the same conclusion independently — but until that moment he waited. He did not want to present before he was ready.

Mendel conducted experiments for eight years at the Augustinian monastery in Brno, with approximately 28,000 pea plants. Published in 1866. No promotion, no networking. Distributed forty copies — the whereabouts of two are known. The monastery's bishop laughed. Mendel died in 1884. The laws of heredity were rediscovered in 1900 — sixteen years later. Performance anxiety was zero; because the audience was zero.

"Montaigne retreated to a monastery tower in 1571 at age 38. Goal: to read and think about himself. The word 'Essais' means 'attempt, trial' in French. 'I educate myself, not the reader.' Reaching a definitive conclusion was not the goal; remaining inside the problem was the goal."

Knowing the Limits · Legitimacy of Attention

The best result reachable is not the best result possible. "There is always someone better than you" — this is realism. There is a line between seizing attention and earning it. Seizing it through needle-pricking — fake wonder, dramatic packaging, jargon inflation — steals attention. Earning it by making the other person live the problem earns it honestly.

"Unintended consequences of being protected from the consequences of your decisions: people who are insulated from the effects of their decisions do not learn."

Nassim Nicholas Taleb · Skin in the Game

Barry Marshall drank a solution containing H. pylori in 1984. Within eight days he had developed gastritis. He proved that stomach ulcers are bacterial in origin. 2005 Nobel Prize in Medicine. Having skin in the game was not a metaphor here — it was literal.

Saying "I used AI" says nothing. Which AI, how, for what purpose — these are the determining questions.

Type Matters: Chat vs Agent

Chat converses; agents research. Chat cannot access the sources it draws on; an agent goes out to the web, reads documents, verifies sources. What we used in this document: an agent system. Night on Earth scenes, Wallraff's book, Grandin's work — the agent scanned, found, cross-checked.

Parallel Research

This document's research into Night on Earth scenes, "entering the subject" examples, and the evidence base for other principles was conducted with three separate research threads simultaneously. A single researcher would do this sequentially — hours, perhaps days. Done in parallel.

No Language Barrier

Accessing Italian, Finnish, French sources used to require knowing the language. AI removes this barrier. If a Japanese study needs to be researched tomorrow, no language barrier. Domain knowledge is being democratized.

Limits

The "this won't hold" intuition of someone who has spent thirty years in courtroom corridors is not present in AI. AI can scan sources, but it cannot know where and at what cost experience was earned. Final judgment belongs to the human.

Observation · Anonymous

I knew a lawyer. Thirty years in courtroom corridors. Before writing a brief, they would spend hours drawing maps — they wouldn't sit down to write without making the relationships visible. The writing took an hour; the mapping stage took days.

They approached research with the same logic. When they began working with AI, the only thing they needed to learn was this: not to give commands, but to talk. Not "research this" but "think through this problem with me." A two-word difference — but the essence of the method.

What do they do now? Sit with their coffee, talk through a problem. The AI researches, presents, debates. They read and correct — "this is wrong," "go from here," "expand this." Research that used to take weeks appears in a few hours.

This document was written applying its own principles to itself.

Reading this document, it is impossible not to notice: we are researching the research method. And we are running this research hand in hand with AI. This is not coincidence — it is self-application.

Primary source priority: Night on Earth's scenes were researched from the film itself, not summaries. Verification reflex: the "Brad Pitt" claim was rejected, DiCaprio verified. Language fidelity: every concept was first constructed in its own terms. Iterative progress: v0.1, v0.2, v0.3, v0.4, v0.5 — earlier versions sit in the git repository.

On AI use, this document also shows its own practice — it does not merely describe, it does. The document is both object and subject.

Doors AI Opens

⊡Scope

Sources in five languages scanned in the same session. Impossible for a single researcher.

⊞Expertise Map

Starting map of a field you know nothing about — key figures, debates, turning points.

⊟Cross-Check

A claim checked against three separate sources simultaneously. Invaluable for the verification reflex.

◈Thinking Partner

An answer to "I'm saying this — where could I be wrong?" Academics call this an advisory board.

◉Synthesis

After fifty sources are read, extracting common patterns. AI makes patterns visible; human judgment decides if they're meaningful.

◊Memory

A correction made at the start of a session is still valid at the end. Humans forget; agents don't.

§ Domain Sub-Principles

Field-Specific Additional Principles

⚖ Legal Research

  • Primary source = statutory text + court decision
  • Citation verification mandatory — case number + full text
  • Currency control — is the statute still in force?
  • Defense of convoluted language: dense clauses are scoping tools, not just complexity
  • Distinction between law in books and living law
  • Open sources: primary court databases, official gazette, legislation portals

✎ Language & Translation

  • Language fidelity principle at center
  • Constant monitoring for source-language pull
  • Preserving fractures is also meaning transfer — Corky example
  • Night on Earth: cultural layer note for every segment
  • No forcing into a single template
  • Concrete context principle — words emptied by abstraction

◈ AI-Assisted Research

  • Use agent system — not chat
  • Verify every produced claim — hallucination risk exists
  • Parallel research threads — independent questions simultaneously
  • Context preservation: corrections still valid at session end
  • Final judgment belongs to the human — intuition absent in AI
  • Model knowledge cutoff date must be checked
