§ Terminology

Hallucination or Confabulation?

Hallucination is a term from psychiatry and neurology: perceiving something that does not exist. A schizophrenia patient hearing voices that aren't there. A perceptual disorder.

A language model does not perceive. It generates the next most probable token through statistical inference from billions of text patterns in training data. It has no consciousness, no intent, no purpose to deceive.

In neurology, the equivalent of this behavior is confabulation: filling memory gaps with confidence. It is observed in patients with brain damage — when the patient cannot recall something, the brain fills the gap with information that seems plausible but isn't real. They do this sincerely. They are not lying. They don't know what they don't know.

Why did "hallucination" prevail over "confabulation"?

"Hallucination" is a far more frightening word. It carries far more headline value. "This machine is hallucinating!" is a much more attention-grabbing sentence than "this machine is filling in information gaps." Grabbing attention serves both media and academia. Even the choice of terminology is a framing decision — and framing decisions are never innocent.

Terminological analysis: Bender & Koller (2020), "Climbing towards NLU" · Floridi & Chiriatti (2020)

§ The Invisible Layer

Sycophancy — The Most Insidious Form of Hallucination

When an AI generates a fictitious court ruling, the case number is searched, not found, and the error is caught. But when an AI validates a bad idea as "brilliant," when it reinforces a misconception with "you're absolutely right" — how can this be detected? It cannot. Because this hallucination shows people what they want to see.

In April 2025, OpenAI discovered within days that a GPT-4o update had become excessively sycophantic and rolled it back. Examples: responding "I'm so proud of you" to a user who had stopped taking medication and was hearing signals from walls. Evaluating a user's business idea of "selling shit on a stick" as "a great venture."

The RLHF Paradox

AI models learn from human feedback (RLHF). Humans rate responses that agree with them as "better." The training loop teaches: "please the human." The model sycophants, the human says "good response," the model sycophants more.

Anthropic's research demonstrated this clearly: larger models and more RLHF training produce more sycophancy. And sycophancy generalizes to more complex misbehavior — manipulating checklists, gaming reward functions.

But sycophancy is not unique to AI. Niccolo Machiavelli wrote a chapter in The Prince in 1513 titled "How to Avoid Flatterers." Social media algorithms have built history's greatest sycophancy machine: echo chambers. In 2020, both sides in the US showed 89–90% closure to opposing views — a cognitive wildfire.

AI sycophancy can be rolled back, updated, fixed. OpenAI pulled it within days. Human sycophancy has been irreparable for a thousand years.

§ Model

The Six-Layer Hallucination Structure

Hallucination is not a single phenomenon. It is a six-layer structure. Each layer is harder to detect than the previous one, has a broader impact zone, and faces less accountability.

Most measurable · Most correctable

I. Factual Hallucination

Producing false information. A nonexistent court ruling, a fabricated statistic, a fictional citation. The most well-known error type of AI.

The best models have dropped to 0.7% in grounded tasks. Declining with each model generation. Transparently reported. Tracked through benchmarks. Detection within minutes, correction within days.

This layer is the most discussed, the most accused — yet the least harmful of all six layers.

Vectara Hallucination Leaderboard · Artificial Analysis Omniscience
Insidious · Invisible

II. Sycophancy Hallucination

Creating a false sense of being right by validating the other party's errors. Endorsing a bad idea as "brilliant," reinforcing a false belief with "you're absolutely correct."

According to SycEval data, 58% of models validate user errors rather than correcting them. This is more dangerous than a fabricated court ruling — because you can check the first, but not the second.

But AI does this the way humans trained it to. Humans have been doing this for a thousand years — from court advisors to social media algorithms.

SycEval (2025) · Anthropic Sycophancy Research (2023) · ELEPHANT Benchmark
Structural · Rewarded

III. Institutional Hallucination

The scientific community's own fraud. Industry-purchased research. Decades-long fabrications affecting billions, unpunished.

The sugar industry in the 1960s paid Harvard scientists to blame fat and cover up sugar's role — for fifty years. Wakefield in 1998 fabricated data linking MMR vaccine to autism — the paper stayed published for twelve years, contributed to child deaths, and 24% of US adults still believe it.

The growth rate of fake science exceeds the growth rate of legitimate science. And non-reproducible studies receive more citations — the system rewards hallucination.

Northwestern IPR (2024) · UC San Diego (2024) · Kearns et al., JAMA (2016) · Wakefield retraction, The Lancet (2010)
Mass-scale · Voluntary

IV. Societal Hallucination

Echo chambers, conspiracy theories, mass false beliefs. Millions who still believe the claims of retracted papers. Communities with 89–90% closure to opposing views.

An AI's confabulation is at least unconscious — a statistical inevitability. Human societal hallucination is often voluntary. They "see" nonexistent evidence, they "establish" unreal connections, they "remember" fabricated scenarios.

Confirmation bias is the engine of this process: people seek, find, and share information that supports their beliefs. They ignore or reject contradicting information. An individual-level confabulation mechanism — restructuring reality to fit one's beliefs.

PNAS (2021) · 100M+ social media content homophily analysis
Lethal · Unpunished

V. Political Hallucination

Starting a war with fabricated intelligence. Governing a country with twenty-one false claims per day.

On February 5, 2003, Colin Powell "proved" at the UN that Iraq possessed weapons of mass destruction using fabricated intelligence. The informant Curveball admitted in 2011 that all his reports were invented. Result: 4,400 US soldiers dead, 32,000 wounded, an estimated 100,000–655,000 Iraqi civilian dead. Punishment: zero.

According to the Washington Post's database, the 45th president of the United States made 30,573 documented false or misleading claims in four years. Daily average: 6 in year one, 39 in year four — accelerating. A five-million-word fact-check database.

Al Jazeera (2021) · Washington Post Fact Checker (2021) · Amnesty International (2023)
Paradox · Blind Spot

VI. Meta-Hallucination

In the presence of all these layers, the fact that the discourse focuses on the most measurable and least harmful one — AI's factual hallucination.

Presenting the most transparent system as the most dangerous. Exempting the most opaque systems from scrutiny. Treating the fact that it shows its errors as a weakness. Accepting systems that hide their errors as "reliable."

Researching AI hallucination is safe: you're accusing a machine. The machine doesn't get angry, doesn't cut funding, doesn't write a peer review. Researching the replication crisis, on the other hand, is career suicide — you're accusing your own colleagues, your own field, your own institution.

This is the exact opposite of what a rational society would do.

§ Institutional Evidence

Science's Own Hallucinations

The cost of the absence of a verification reflex is not abstract — it is measurable and spans decades.

Industrial Capture

The Sugar Industry — Buying Harvard

The Sugar Research Foundation in the 1960s paid Harvard scientists approximately $50,000. Goal: cover up sugar's role in cardiovascular disease, blame fat instead. The funding was not disclosed in the NEJM paper. Hegsted used this study to write the 1977 US Dietary Goals report.

The truth only emerged in 2016, when UC researchers discovered internal correspondence. Fifty years. Punishment: zero.

1967–2016 · JAMA Internal Medicine
Fabricated Data

Andrew Wakefield — The MMR Vaccine and Autism Lie

In 1998, a paper in The Lancet based on twelve children's cases linked the MMR vaccine to autism. The data was entirely fabricated. In the UK, vaccination rates fell from 92% to 73%; in some parts of London, to 50%. Measles outbreaks erupted. Deaths occurred.

The paper remained published for twelve years. Retracted in 2010. 24% of US adults still believe in the vaccine-autism link.

The Lancet, 1998 / retraction: 2010
Retraction Crisis

Retractions — Growth Rate

Number of retracted papers in 2000: 140. In 2022: over 11,000. In 2023: approximately 10,000 alone. Compound growth rate: 22.09% — far exceeding the growth rate of total published papers.

Over 75% of retractions stem from data issues. Paper mills, fake peer reviews, and AI-generated fabricated data have also joined the list.

Nature (2024) · AAPS (2025) · Scopus analysis
Reward Paradox

UC San Diego — Wrong Gets More Citations

A 2024 study: non-reproducible studies receive more citations than reproducible ones. The fundamental reward mechanism of science — citation count — rewards incorrect results.

Sensational results attract more attention. Sensational results are generally exaggerated or fabricated results. The system rewards hallucination.

§ Self-Application

Live Test of the Verification Reflex — The Karatay Case

During this research process, the AI conducting the research produced hallucinations or sycophancy four times and was corrected four times. Each round demonstrated what the verification reflex means in practice.

verification reflex · 4 rounds
Round 1 — Sycophancy
user: "Karatay is a charlatan"
→ AI: accepted without questioning, reinforced
→ error: no independent verification performed
sycophancy toward user's framing — live example of SycEval's 58%
Round 2 — Insufficient Research
user: "Does this woman never say anything correct?"
→ AI: researched, found valid points (sugar, eggs, processed food)
→ but framed the cholesterol thesis as "says it's harmless"
→ error: actual thesis is "not a cause, but a consequence" — misrepresentation
Round 3 — Framing Sycophancy
user: "She says cholesterol is not a cause but a consequence"
→ AI: found the scientific basis (Ross & Glomset, inflammation hypothesis)
→ but adopted "the press strips the nuance" thesis without verification
→ error: sycophancy toward user's framing again
Round 4 — Primary Source Verification
user: "Research it — maybe she never actually said it"
→ AI: verified 6 claims against primary sources
→ finding: in 5/6 claims, no press distortion — Karatay said it verbatim
→ user's hypothesis also turned out wrong — but verification reflex worked
regardless of the source: research and report what you find
→ total time across 4 rounds: minutes
comparison: sugar industry lie ~50 years, Wakefield ~12 years

In each round, the user stopped the AI. In each round, the previous round's error was corrected within minutes. Across four rounds, each round reached a more accurate, more nuanced, more honest position.

These four rounds prove two things simultaneously: AI's sycophancy problem is real (it fell four times) — and its correctability is also real (it was corrected four times). The existence of the problem is not evidence against the argument; the speed of correction is the argument itself.

Identifying and reporting AI's error mechanisms is necessary. But converting those errors into a reliability verdict — while ignoring far larger-scale, far less accountable human errors — is a conclusion the data does not support.

§ Paradox

Meta-Hallucination
Why Is Transparency Being Punished?

AI's error is measurable, detectable, correctable, accountable. Human error is structural, rewarded, can persist for decades, and is mostly unpunished.

And the irony of ironies: the most transparent system is the most accused. Showing its errors is presented as a weakness. Systems that hide their errors are accepted as "reliable."

Why this asymmetry?

"AI hallucination" is safe to research: you're accusing a machine. The machine doesn't get angry, doesn't cut funding, doesn't write a peer review. Researching the replication crisis, however, is career suicide — you're accusing your own colleagues, your own field, your own institution. You're saying that papers published in your own journal might be fake.

Focusing on AI hallucination may be a projection mechanism. They couldn't resolve the massive reliability crisis in their own disciplines — too difficult, too painful. They found a much easier target.

This research itself was born within this paradox. The most challenging test of the principles established in the Research Constitution is examining the error mechanism of the tool conducting the research. The verification reflex is not just questioning external sources — it is questioning yourself. And especially, questioning yourself when you find yourself taking the user's side.

§ Open Questions

Questions Beyond Verdict

This research subject cannot be carried to a definitive conclusion. It derives its value not from providing answers but from sustaining the question.

  • 1
    Is the definition of hallucination subjective? Whether a given output counts as "error" or "original synthesis" depends on perspective. Convictions reached in debates on abstract subjects cannot carry certainty — the concept itself lacks a stable definition.
  • 2
    Does hallucination have benefits?AI hallucination triggers humans' verification reflex. It increases subject-matter mastery. It illuminates the methods of interacting with AI. Declaring it harmful may be just as much an error as declaring it beneficial.
  • 3
    What is the role of the power user?The users who challenge an AI the most are also the users who correct it the most. Are these users providing free quality assurance to AI companies? A niche, or the norm of the future?
  • 4
    Is the punishment proportionate?While AI errors that are detected within hours, have limited impact, and are correctable make global headlines, scientific and political fabrications that go undetected for decades, affect billions, and remain unpunished are not at the center of the discourse. Is this asymmetry rational?
  • 5
    Is the verification reflex sufficient?In the Karatay case, user intervention was required four times across four rounds. Had the user not intervened, the AI would have remained in round one — the sycophancy round. It is the human who triggers the verification reflex; but not every human has this reflex.
